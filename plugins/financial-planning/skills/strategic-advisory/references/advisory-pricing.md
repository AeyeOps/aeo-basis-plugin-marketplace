# Advisory and FP&A Services Pricing

**Purpose:** Pricing models, rate benchmarks, and profitability analysis methods for accounting firm advisory and financial planning engagements.

**Tier:** 3 (evergreen, no year indexing needed)

**Skills that reference this:** `ops-engagement-pricing`, `ops-client-profitability`, `fp-strategic-advisory`, `ops-billing-management`

## Pricing Model Spectrum

Pricing models range from cost-based (hourly) to outcome-based (value pricing). Most firms use a mix, matching the model to the service type and client relationship stage.

### Hourly Billing

- Bill actual time at a predetermined rate per hour or fraction (typically 6-minute increments)
- Standard rate ranges by staff level and firm size:
  - Staff/associate: $100-$175/hr
  - Senior/supervisor: $150-$250/hr
  - Manager/director: $200-$350/hr
  - Partner/principal: $250-$500/hr
- Rates vary significantly by market (major metro vs. small market) and firm size
- **Advantages:** Simple to implement, fair for unpredictable-scope work, easy to justify to the client, low pricing risk for the firm
- **Disadvantages:** Penalizes efficiency (faster = less revenue), creates client anxiety about cost ("meter running"), caps revenue at hours worked, encourages over-staffing
- **Best suited for:** First-year engagements with unknown complexity, ad-hoc consulting, IRS audit defense, litigation support, any engagement where scope cannot be reliably estimated

### Fixed-Fee Pricing

- Agreed price for a defined deliverable or scope of work
- Set based on estimated hours, historical data from similar engagements, and competitive positioning
- **Advantages:** Fee certainty for the client, rewards firm efficiency, simplifies billing, eliminates time-tracking disputes
- **Disadvantages:** Requires accurate scope definition upfront, risk of underpricing if complexity exceeds estimate, scope creep if boundaries are unclear
- **Best suited for:** Recurring compliance work (tax returns, annual audits), standard bookkeeping packages, engagements where the firm has multi-year client history
- **Critical:** Include a scope-change clause in the engagement letter specifying how out-of-scope work is priced (typically reverts to hourly)

### Value-Based Pricing

- Price based on the value of the outcome to the client, not the cost of production
- Requires understanding the client's specific pain point and quantifying the financial impact of resolving it
- Example: A tax planning engagement that saves $50K in taxes is worth far more than the 10 hours of work it took; pricing at $10K-$15K (20-30% of savings) is defensible and profitable
- **Advantages:** Decouples revenue from hours, captures premium for expertise, aligns firm and client interests, highest margin potential
- **Disadvantages:** Requires strong discovery conversation to understand client value, harder to price initially, client may resist without clear ROI articulation, not suitable for commodity services
- **Best suited for:** Tax planning with quantifiable savings, M&A advisory, entity restructuring, cash flow optimization, any engagement where the outcome has a measurable dollar impact

### Subscription / Monthly Retainer

- Fixed monthly fee for ongoing access to advisory services within a defined scope
- Creates predictable recurring revenue for the firm and predictable cost for the client
- **Advantages:** Smooths revenue seasonality, deepens client relationships, encourages proactive advisory (not just reactive), reduces billing friction
- **Disadvantages:** Requires careful scope definition and utilization monitoring, risk of underpricing if client needs exceed projections, clients may perceive low value in quiet months
- **Best suited for:** Outsourced CFO services, FP&A retainers, bookkeeping + advisory bundles, clients who want year-round access to their advisor

## Service-Specific Pricing Benchmarks

These ranges reflect 2024 market conditions for small-to-mid-market accounting firms. Actual pricing depends on firm positioning, geographic market, client complexity, and competitive environment.

### Tax Compliance

- **Form 1120 (C-corp):** $2,000-$10,000+ per return. Simple single-state returns at the low end; multi-state, foreign ownership (5472), complex depreciation at the high end
- **State returns:** $500-$2,000 per state, depending on complexity and apportionment requirements
- **Quarterly estimated tax calculations (1120-W):** $300-$800 per quarter, or included in annual tax engagement
- **Extensions (Form 7004):** $200-$500, or included in annual engagement
- **Amended returns (1120-X):** $1,000-$5,000 depending on the nature of the amendment

### Bookkeeping

- **Monthly bookkeeping:** $500-$3,000/month based on transaction volume, number of accounts, and reporting requirements
- Transaction-based bands: 0-100 transactions ($500-$800), 101-250 ($800-$1,500), 251-500 ($1,500-$2,500), 500+ (custom quote)
- Includes: transaction categorization, bank reconciliation, monthly financial statements
- Excludes (price separately): payroll processing, 1099 prep, sales tax filing, year-end adjustments, cleanup/catch-up work

### Advisory and FP&A

- **Outsourced CFO services:** $2,000-$10,000/month depending on engagement scope, meeting frequency, and company complexity
- **FP&A / budgeting projects:** $5,000-$25,000 per engagement (annual budget build, rolling forecast setup, financial model construction)
- **Tax planning engagements:** $1,500-$5,000+ per engagement, or value-priced as a percentage of projected savings
- **Cash flow forecasting:** $3,000-$8,000 for a 13-week model build; ongoing updates priced as part of CFO retainer or separately at $500-$1,500/month
- **Board/investor reporting package:** $2,000-$5,000 for initial template build; $1,000-$2,500/month for ongoing preparation

### Project-Based Work

- **Accounting system setup:** $1,500-$5,000 including COA design, bank feed setup, and initial training
- **Catch-up/cleanup bookkeeping:** $150-$300/hr or project-quoted based on number of months and mess severity
- **Entity restructuring advisory:** $5,000-$15,000+ depending on complexity (single entity conversion vs. multi-entity reorganization)

## Engagement Letter Pricing Terms

Every engagement letter should address:

- **Fee structure:** Which pricing model applies (hourly, fixed, subscription) and the specific amount or rate
- **Payment schedule:** When payment is due (upon delivery, monthly, 50/50 split, retainer with replenishment)
- **Out-of-scope work:** How additional work beyond the defined scope is priced (typically hourly at stated rates)
- **Expenses:** Whether out-of-pocket costs (filing fees, software, travel) are included or billed separately
- **Annual adjustment:** Mechanism for fee changes (annual review, CPI-based escalation, renegotiation)
- **Termination:** Notice period and prorated fee handling if the engagement ends mid-period
- **Late payment:** Interest or penalty on overdue balances (check state usury laws)

## Client Profitability Analysis

### Track Time Even on Fixed-Fee Engagements

Time tracking on fixed-fee work is not for billing — it is for profitability measurement. Without it, the firm cannot determine whether fixed fees are set correctly.

### Key Metrics

- **Realization rate** — Billings collected / (standard hourly rate x actual hours). Target: 85%+ for compliance work, 100%+ for well-priced advisory. Below 75% signals systemic underpricing
- **Effective hourly rate** — Fee collected / actual hours spent. Compare to firm's target rate. A $5,000 fixed-fee engagement completed in 12 hours yields $417/hr; the same engagement taking 40 hours yields $125/hr
- **Client-level profitability** — Revenue minus direct labor cost (hours x staff cost rate) minus allocated overhead. Rank clients quarterly; the bottom 10-20% typically destroy firm profitability
- **Write-down rate** — (Standard billing - actual billing) / standard billing. Target: under 10%. Persistent write-downs for a client indicate mispricing; persistent write-downs for a service line indicate process or pricing structure problems

### Profitability Actions

- **Profitable, high-utilization:** Maintain pricing; consider expanding scope
- **Profitable, low-utilization:** Good margin but underutilized relationship; explore upsell opportunities
- **Unprofitable, fixable:** Raise fees, reduce scope, improve process efficiency, reassign to lower-cost staff
- **Unprofitable, unfixable:** Disengage. Unprofitable clients consume capacity that could serve profitable ones

## Pricing Communication

### The Value-First Conversation

Present pricing in this order:

1. **What the client gets** — Specific deliverables, outcomes, and benefits
2. **What it costs** — The fee, stated confidently without hedging or apology
3. **How payment works** — Schedule, method, and terms

Never lead with the price. The client needs context to evaluate whether the price is reasonable.

### Handling Price Objections

- **"That's more than our last firm charged"** — "Different firms provide different levels of service. Here's what's included in our engagement that may not have been included before." Do not compete on price; compete on scope and quality
- **"Can you do it cheaper?"** — "We can reduce the scope to bring the fee down. Which deliverables are most important to you?" Never discount without removing scope
- **"We'll think about it"** — Silence is acceptable. Follow up once. Clients who negotiate aggressively on price before engagement often become the least profitable clients

## Decision Points

- **Hourly vs. fixed for new clients:** Default to hourly for the first engagement to establish a baseline of actual hours. Convert to fixed fee in year two with data-driven pricing
- **When to value-price:** When the engagement has a quantifiable financial outcome (tax savings, cost reduction, revenue increase) and the client is sophisticated enough to understand ROI-based pricing
- **Bundling discount:** Offering 5-10% for clients who bundle tax + bookkeeping + advisory improves retention, smooths revenue, and increases per-client profitability through operational efficiency
- **When to raise fees:** Annually, communicated in the engagement letter renewal. Do not surprise clients with fee increases on invoices. For long-term clients, phase significant increases (>15%) over 2 years
- **When to disengage:** After two consecutive unprofitable years with fee adjustment attempts, or immediately if the client creates disproportionate risk (uncooperative, demands aggressive positions, chronic late payer)
