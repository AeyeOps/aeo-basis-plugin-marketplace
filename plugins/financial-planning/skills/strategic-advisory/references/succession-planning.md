## Business Valuation Approaches

Three primary approaches establish fair market value (FMV) for closely-held C-corps. IRS Revenue Ruling 59-60 remains the foundational authority, requiring consideration of all relevant factors including nature of the business, economic outlook, book value, earning capacity, dividend-paying capacity, goodwill, prior sales of stock, and market price of comparable companies.

### Asset-Based Approach

- **Book value:** Net assets per the balance sheet (total assets minus total liabilities). Simplest method; rarely reflects true FMV because GAAP book values diverge from market values
- **Adjusted net asset value:** Restate each asset and liability to current FMV. Requires independent appraisals of real property, equipment, and intangibles. Appropriate for asset-intensive businesses or holding companies
- Best suited for: companies with substantial tangible assets, investment holding entities, businesses being liquidated

### Income-Based Approach

- **Capitalization of earnings:** Divide normalized earnings by an appropriate capitalization rate. Cap rate reflects risk — higher risk = higher cap rate = lower value. Typical cap rates for small closely-held companies: 15-30%
- **Discounted cash flow (DCF):** Project future free cash flows over a discrete period (typically 5-10 years), then discount to present value using a weighted average cost of capital (WACC). Terminal value captures value beyond the projection period
- Normalization adjustments: remove owner compensation above market, one-time expenses, related-party transactions at non-market rates, and discretionary expenses
- Best suited for: operating businesses with established earnings history, professional service firms, businesses valued as going concerns

### Market-Based Approach

- **Comparable transactions:** Analyze sale prices of similar privately-held businesses. Sources: BizBuySell, Pratt's Stats, IBBA market databases
- **Guideline public companies:** Identify publicly traded companies in the same industry and apply their valuation multiples (P/E, EV/EBITDA, revenue multiples) with appropriate size and liquidity adjustments
- Best suited for: industries with active transaction markets, businesses where comparable data is available

## Valuation Discounts

Discounts adjust FMV to reflect the actual position of the interest being valued. IRS scrutinizes stacked discounts aggressively; each must be independently supportable.

- **Discount for lack of marketability (DLOM):** Reflects the absence of a ready market for closely-held stock. Typical range: 15-35%. Supported by restricted stock studies (Silber, FMV Opinions) and pre-IPO studies (Willamette Management)
- **Discount for lack of control / minority interest:** Applies when the interest being valued cannot unilaterally direct company operations, declare dividends, or liquidate. Typical range: 15-25%. Derived from control premium studies (Mergerstat)
- **Key person discount:** Reflects value concentration in a specific individual whose departure would impair earnings. Quantified by estimating revenue/profit at risk if the key person departs. Typical range: 5-25%
- **Aggregate discount caps:** Combined discounts exceeding 40-45% attract IRS challenge. Ensure each discount is empirically supported and not duplicative

## Buy-Sell Agreements

A buy-sell agreement is the legal mechanism that governs ownership transfer upon a triggering event. Every closely-held C-corp with more than one shareholder should have one.

### Agreement Types

- **Cross-purchase:** Remaining shareholders buy the departing shareholder's stock individually. Buyers get a stepped-up basis in acquired shares. Becomes unwieldy with more than 3-4 shareholders (each pair needs separate insurance policies)
- **Entity redemption (stock redemption):** The corporation buys back the departing shareholder's stock. Simpler to administer with multiple shareholders. Risk: IRC Section 318 attribution rules may recharacterize redemption as a dividend if remaining shareholders are related parties
- **Hybrid / wait-and-see:** Agreement defers the decision on who buys (entity or individuals) until a trigger event occurs. Provides flexibility; the entity has first right of refusal, then remaining shareholders

### Funding Mechanisms

- **Life insurance:** Most common for death-triggered buyouts. Cross-purchase uses individual-owned policies; entity redemption uses corporate-owned policies. Consider C-corp AMT exposure on corporate-owned life insurance (COLI) proceeds
- **Installment notes:** Seller finances the buyout over time (typically 5-10 years). IRC Section 453 governs installment sale treatment. Interest rate must meet applicable federal rate (AFR) minimum to avoid imputed interest under Section 7872
- **Sinking fund:** Corporation sets aside funds annually in a dedicated account. Advantage: no insurance underwriting required. Disadvantage: accumulated funds are a corporate asset exposed to creditors

### Trigger Events

Death, permanent disability, retirement at specified age, voluntary termination, involuntary termination for cause, divorce (to prevent ex-spouse from becoming a shareholder), bankruptcy of a shareholder

## Ownership Transition Paths

### Internal Sale to Family or Employees

- Installment sale: spreads gain recognition; buyer uses future earnings to fund payments
- Gift/sale combination: gift a portion (using annual exclusion and/or lifetime exemption), sell the remainder at FMV. Reduces total purchase price while transferring value
- Grantor retained annuity trust (GRAT): owner transfers stock to a trust, retains annuity payments for a term, remainder passes to beneficiaries at reduced gift tax cost if asset appreciation exceeds the Section 7520 rate

### Management Buyout (MBO)

- Management team acquires the business, typically with a combination of equity contribution, seller financing, and third-party debt
- Tax consideration: if structured as a stock purchase, the corporation's basis in its assets carries over (no step-up). Consider a Section 338(h)(10) election to treat as an asset sale if the tax math favors it

### Employee Stock Ownership Plan (ESOP)

- Tax-qualified retirement plan that invests primarily in employer stock
- C-corp advantage: selling shareholder can defer gain under IRC Section 1042 if ESOP holds 30%+ of outstanding stock after the sale and proceeds are reinvested in qualified replacement property within 12 months
- Corporation deducts contributions used to repay ESOP acquisition debt (both principal and interest), creating a tax-efficient financing structure

### Third-Party Sale

- Maximize value through competitive bidding process
- Due diligence preparation: clean financials (3-5 years audited or reviewed), resolved contingencies, documented processes, transferable contracts and relationships
- Representations and warranties insurance can bridge valuation gaps

### Liquidation

- Last resort; least tax-efficient for C-corps due to double taxation (corporate-level gain on asset disposition + shareholder-level gain on liquidating distributions under IRC Section 331)

## Estate and Gift Tax Implications

### Special Valuation Rules (Sections 2701-2704)

- **Section 2701:** Applies to transfers of junior equity interests (common stock) when senior interests (preferred stock) are retained. Retained interests valued at zero unless they meet "qualified payment" requirements, potentially inflating the taxable gift
- **Section 2704:** Restricts the ability to claim valuation discounts attributable to lapsing voting or liquidation rights in family-controlled entities. Proposed regulations (never finalized) would have further limited discounts

### Exemption Amounts

- **Annual gift exclusion:** $18,000 per donee (2024), $19,000 per donee (2025)
- **Lifetime gift/estate tax exemption:** $13.61M per individual (2024), $13.99M per individual (2025)
- **TCJA sunset risk:** If the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act provisions expire after 2025, the lifetime exemption reverts to approximately $7M (indexed). This creates urgency for large transfers before year-end 2025
- **Anti-clawback rule:** Treasury Reg. 20.2010-1(c) confirms that gifts made using the higher exemption will not be clawed back if the exemption later decreases

## Key Person Risk Assessment

- **Identify critical individuals:** Owners, rainmakers, technical specialists, relationship holders
- **Quantify dependency:** What percentage of revenue, client relationships, or institutional knowledge is concentrated in one person? Dependency above 30% on any individual is high-risk
- **Mitigation strategies:** Cross-training, documented procedures, non-compete/non-solicitation agreements, key person life and disability insurance, management depth development
- **Insurance sizing:** Key person life insurance face amount should cover estimated revenue loss during a transition period (typically 2-3 years of the person's attributed revenue or earnings contribution)

## Tax Considerations for C-Corp Transitions

- **Section 338(h)(10) election:** Buyer and seller jointly elect to treat a stock acquisition as a deemed asset sale. Buyer gets stepped-up basis in assets; seller reports as if assets were sold. Beneficial when asset basis step-up value exceeds the additional tax cost to the seller
- **Section 1202 QSBS:** Potentially excludes up to 100% of gain on qualified small business stock held 5+ years. Not available for C-corps with aggregate gross assets exceeding $50M at any time after August 10, 1993
- **Accumulated earnings tax (Section 531):** Corporations accumulating earnings beyond reasonable business needs face a 20% penalty tax. Transition planning (building cash reserves for a buyout) is generally a reasonable business need, but must be documented with board resolutions and a formal succession plan
- **Section 302 redemption:** Stock redemption must qualify as "substantially disproportionate" or "not essentially equivalent to a dividend" to receive capital gain treatment. Attribution rules under Section 318 are the primary trap

## Decision Points

- **When to start:** Succession planning horizon is typically 3-7 years for optimal tax and operational outcomes. Beginning earlier preserves more options and allows gradual, tax-efficient transfers
- **Valuation approach selection:** Asset-based for holding companies or asset-heavy businesses; income-based for operating companies with earnings history; market-based when comparable data exists. Most engagements use two or more approaches and reconcile
- **Buy-sell structure:** Cross-purchase when few shareholders and basis step-up matters; entity redemption when many shareholders or administrative simplicity is prioritized; hybrid when flexibility is paramount
- **Stock vs. asset sale:** Model both structures for the specific transaction. Section 338(h)(10) bridges the gap when buyer wants asset treatment and seller holds stock
- **Gift vs. sell:** Gift when lifetime exemption is available and TCJA sunset risk is present; sell when the owner needs proceeds for retirement funding

## Accounting System Integration Notes

- **Shareholder equity tracking:** Use equity-type accounts to track each shareholder's contributed capital and retained earnings allocation. Subaccounts by shareholder enable per-owner reporting. Invoke `qbo-integration:qbo-coa` for platform-specific equity account setup
- **Redemption recording:** Stock redemptions reduce the appropriate equity account and debit cash or note payable. Do not run through income accounts
- **Installment note tracking:** Record seller-financed notes as long-term liabilities. Use scheduled transactions for recurring principal and interest payments. Interest expense is deductible; principal is not
- **Key person insurance:** Record premium payments as an expense (non-deductible for tax; deductible for book if the corporation is beneficiary). Track cash surrender value as an other asset on the balance sheet
- **Valuation documentation:** Attach valuation reports and buy-sell agreements to the company record in the accounting system (or linked document management) for audit trail continuity
