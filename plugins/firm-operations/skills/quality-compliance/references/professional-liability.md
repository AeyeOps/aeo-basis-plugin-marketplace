# Professional Liability

**Purpose:** Document professional liability (E&O) risk management for accounting firms, covering common claim triggers, loss prevention practices, and insurance considerations.

**Skills that reference this:** risk-management, engagement-quality, client-screening

---

## Professional Liability Insurance Overview

- Professional liability insurance (Errors & Omissions / E&O) covers claims arising from the performance of professional accounting services
- Coverage typically includes: defense costs, settlements, judgments for negligent acts, errors, or omissions
- Major CPA professional liability carriers: CAMICO, AICPA Professional Liability Program (underwritten by Aon/CNA), CPAI (CPA Insurance), state society programs
- Policy types: claims-made (most common for CPA E&O) vs. occurrence-based
- Claims-made policies cover claims reported during the policy period, regardless of when the act occurred, subject to the retroactive date
- Tail coverage (extended reporting period) is critical when switching carriers or retiring

## Common Claim Triggers

### Tax Practice Claims (Highest Frequency for Small Firms)
- **Missed deadlines:** Failure to file returns or extensions by due dates, resulting in penalties and interest
- **Incorrect tax positions:** Errors in tax computations, missed deductions/credits, or aggressive positions that do not survive audit
- **Failure to advise:** Not informing the client of tax planning opportunities, elections, or consequences of transactions
- **Estimated tax payment errors:** Incorrect calculations leading to underpayment penalties
- **Entity selection/structure advice:** Recommending or failing to recommend entity changes with adverse tax consequences
- **Multi-state nexus issues:** Missing state filing obligations, especially for clients with multistate operations
- **Carryforward/carryback errors:** Losing track of NOLs, capital loss carryforwards, or credit carryforwards

### Bookkeeping and Accounting Claims
- **Inaccurate financial statements:** Errors in classification, reconciliation, or presentation
- **Failure to detect fraud:** Not identifying embezzlement or management fraud (note: bookkeeping is not an audit, but failure to notice obvious red flags can still generate claims)
- **Commingling of duties:** Failing to advise clients on internal controls and segregation of duties
- **Software errors:** Incorrect accounting system setup, wrong account mapping, data entry errors that propagate

### Engagement Management Claims
- **Scope creep:** Performing work outside the engagement letter scope without documenting the expanded scope
- **Unauthorized reliance:** Third parties relying on deliverables not intended for their use
- **Client communication failures:** Not documenting advice given verbally, leading to disputes about what was recommended

## Loss Prevention Practices

### Engagement Letters as the Primary Shield
- The single most effective loss prevention tool (see engagement-letters.md)
- Claims without an engagement letter are significantly harder to defend
- The engagement letter defines scope, limits liability, and establishes dispute resolution
- CAMICO data: claims against firms without engagement letters result in higher payouts and worse outcomes

### Documentation Standards
- **Document everything:** All advice, recommendations, and client decisions documented in writing
- **Contemporaneous notes:** Memo-to-file for verbal conversations containing advice or decisions
- **Email as documentation:** Written follow-up after verbal discussions summarizing what was discussed and agreed
- **Workpaper standards:** Sufficient detail that another qualified professional could understand the work performed and conclusions reached
- **Negative documentation:** When a client declines advice, document the recommendation made, the client's decision to decline, and the potential consequences communicated

### Client Screening and Red Flags
- Prior firm termination (always contact predecessor with client consent)
- History of tax noncompliance, delinquent filings, or unpaid taxes
- Unrealistic expectations about outcomes (especially regarding refunds or aggressive positions)
- Fee sensitivity disproportionate to complexity (client wants complex work for simple-return fees)
- Litigious history against prior professionals
- Inability or unwillingness to provide complete information
- Related-party transaction complexity without supporting documentation
- Foreign ownership/operations requiring Form 5472 or FBAR compliance (higher risk if client is uncooperative)

### Deadline Management
- Centralized deadline tracking system with redundant reminders
- Never rely on a single person to track deadlines
- Automatic extension filing policy: if information is not received by [date], file extension automatically
- Document when information was received and when the return was completed
- Missed deadlines are the most preventable claim trigger

### Peer Review Implications
- Peer review findings can be used as evidence in malpractice litigation
- A pattern of peer review deficiencies suggests systemic quality issues
- Maintaining a clean peer review record is both a quality objective and a liability defense
- Correct deficiencies promptly and document corrective actions

## Statute of Limitations Defense

- Florida statute of limitations for professional malpractice: 2 years from discovery, with a 4-year statute of repose
- Federal tax claims may have longer exposure due to IRS audit timelines (3-year assessment period, 6 years if >25% understatement)
- Claims-made policy coverage must align with potential exposure periods
- Document retention policies should consider statute of limitations periods
- Engagement letters can include a contractual limitations period (e.g., "any claim must be brought within 12 months of delivery") -- enforceability varies by jurisdiction

## Risk Mitigation Strategies

### Proactive Communication
- Confirm receipt of all client-provided documents in writing
- Provide written status updates during extended engagements
- Send year-end planning letters to all clients with action items
- Notify clients promptly of any errors discovered after filing (and corrective actions taken)

### Second-Pair-of-Eyes Review
- All returns and deliverables reviewed by someone other than the preparer before delivery
- Review checklist documents what was examined and any issues identified
- Even sole practitioners should implement a structured self-review process or engage a peer reviewer

### Insurance Coverage Review
- Review coverage annually with the carrier
- Ensure coverage limits are adequate for the firm's client base and engagement types
- Confirm retroactive date covers the firm's full history
- Report potential claims promptly -- late reporting can void coverage
- Understand policy exclusions: intentional acts, criminal conduct, bodily injury, employment practices

### Disengagement Risk
- Disengagement from a client does not eliminate exposure for prior work
- Maintain tail coverage or extended reporting period for departed clients
- Retain workpapers per the document retention policy even after disengagement
- Issue a clear disengagement letter to avoid implied continuation of services

## Decision Points

- **Coverage limits:** Minimum recommended coverage for small tax practices is $500K-$1M per claim, $1M-$2M aggregate. Increase based on client size and engagement complexity.
- **Deductible selection:** Higher deductibles reduce premiums but increase out-of-pocket exposure. Consider the firm's financial capacity to absorb defense costs.
- **Risk acceptance criteria:** Define thresholds for client acceptance (e.g., maximum revenue from any single client, maximum complexity tier without partner involvement).
- **Pre-claim reporting:** Most carriers allow (and encourage) reporting of "circumstances that may give rise to a claim." Early reporting can result in better outcomes through proactive carrier involvement.
