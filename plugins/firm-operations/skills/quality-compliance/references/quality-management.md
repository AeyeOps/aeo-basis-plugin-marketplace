## Key Conceptual Shift: Prescriptive to Risk-Based

The old SQCS framework was prescriptive -- firms followed a checklist of required policies. SQMS No. 1 shifts to a **risk assessment approach**:

1. Identify quality objectives for each component
2. Identify and assess quality risks that threaten those objectives
3. Design and implement responses to address the identified risks
4. Monitor the system and remediate deficiencies

This means two firms of different sizes and service mixes will have different quality management systems, both equally compliant, because their risk profiles differ.

## Eight Components of SQMS No. 1

### 1. Firm's Risk Assessment Process
- The foundation: drives all other components
- Establish quality objectives, identify quality risks, design responses
- Document the rationale for risk assessments
- Reassess when changes occur (new service lines, staff turnover, regulatory changes)

### 2. Governance and Leadership
- Firm leadership sets the tone for quality
- Assign ultimate responsibility and accountability for the QM system
- Establish and communicate firm culture that quality is non-negotiable
- Leadership must commit resources (time, budget, personnel) to QM

### 3. Relevant Ethical Requirements
- Independence monitoring and documentation
- AICPA Code of Professional Conduct compliance
- State board ethical requirements (Florida: Chapter 473, FAC 61H1)
- Annual independence confirmations from all professional staff
- Conflict of interest identification and resolution procedures

### 4. Acceptance and Continuance of Client Relationships and Engagements
- Risk-based client acceptance criteria (see engagement-letters.md)
- Annual continuance evaluation
- Document acceptance/continuance decisions and rationale
- Procedures for withdrawing from engagements when risk is unacceptable

### 5. Engagement Performance
- Engagement-level quality controls: planning, supervision, review
- Consultation policies for complex or unusual matters
- Documentation standards (workpaper requirements)
- Differences of opinion resolution process
- Use of templates and checklists to ensure completeness

### 6. Resources
- **Human resources:** Competency assessment, CPE compliance, assignment based on qualifications
- **Technological resources:** Software validation, IT security, data backup
- **Intellectual resources:** Methodology, templates, research tools, knowledge management
- **Service providers:** Oversight of outsourced functions (e.g., offshore bookkeeping, IT)

### 7. Information and Communication
- Internal communication: policies accessible to all staff, feedback channels
- External communication: regulatory reporting, client communications
- Information systems that support QM (document management, workflow tracking)
- Whistleblower / escalation mechanisms for quality concerns

### 8. Monitoring and Remediation Process
- Ongoing monitoring activities (inspection of completed engagements)
- Annual inspection program: select engagements for internal review
- Root cause analysis for identified deficiencies
- Remediation plans with timelines and accountability
- External feedback: peer review findings, regulatory inquiries, litigation

## Peer Review Alignment

- AICPA Peer Review Standards are being updated to align with SQMS framework
- Peer reviewers will evaluate whether the firm's QM system is designed appropriately and operating effectively
- System reviews will focus on the firm's risk assessment and response cycle, not just engagement-level compliance
- Engagement reviews remain for firms enrolled in the engagement review program
- The first peer review under SQMS will examine the firm's design AND implementation
- Deficiencies in the QM system design (not just engagement execution) can result in peer review findings

## Implementation for Small Firms (1-5 professionals)

### Scaling Principles
- The standard explicitly acknowledges that small firms will have simpler, less formal systems
- A sole practitioner may combine the roles of leadership, engagement partner, and QM monitor
- Documentation can be streamlined but must still exist -- "if it isn't documented, it didn't happen"
- Off-the-shelf QM system templates from AICPA, state societies, or peer review administrators are acceptable starting points

### Practical Minimum for a Small Tax Practice
- Written quality management policies document (10-20 pages)
- Client acceptance/continuance checklist
- Engagement-level review checklist for each return type
- Annual self-inspection of 2-3 completed engagements
- CPE tracking and compliance documentation
- Independence confirmation (even sole practitioners confirm independence from attest clients)
- Annual system evaluation memo

### Common Small-Firm Pitfalls
- No documentation of the risk assessment process (fatal in peer review)
- Client acceptance based solely on fees, ignoring risk factors
- No engagement review process (sole practitioner reviews own work without structured checklist)
- Stale policies that reference superseded standards (still citing SQCS instead of SQMS)

## Documentation Requirements

- Quality management policies manual (written, accessible to all personnel)
- Risk assessment documentation (quality objectives, risks, responses)
- Client acceptance/continuance records
- Engagement-level review evidence (reviewer sign-off, review notes)
- Monitoring/inspection results and remediation actions
- Annual evaluation of the QM system (required within one year of the effective date, then annually)
- Retain QM documentation for the period required by peer review (typically current + prior review cycle)

## Decision Points

- **When to start implementation:** Firms should have had their system designed and implemented by Dec 15, 2025. Firms still operating under SQCS need to transition immediately.
- **Buy vs. build QM system:** Small firms should strongly consider purchasing a template (AICPA, state society, CAMICO) rather than building from scratch.
- **Engagement quality reviewer (EQR):** SQMS No. 2 requires EQR for certain engagements. For tax-only firms, EQR requirements are generally limited unless the firm performs audits or reviews. However, firms should assess whether any tax engagements meet their own criteria for EQR (high-risk, high-profile clients).
