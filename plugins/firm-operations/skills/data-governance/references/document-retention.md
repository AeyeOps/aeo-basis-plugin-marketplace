# Document Retention

**Purpose:** Define retention periods, storage standards, and destruction procedures for accounting firm records, client documents, and workpapers.

**Skills that reference this:** records-management, engagement-archival, compliance-audit

---

## IRS Retention Requirements

### General Rule: Assessment Period
- **3 years:** Standard period of limitations for IRS to assess additional tax (IRC Section 6501(a))
- IRS can audit and assess additional tax within 3 years of the later of: the filing date or the due date
- This is the minimum retention period for tax returns and supporting documentation

### Extended Periods
- **6 years:** If gross income is understated by more than 25% (IRC Section 6501(e))
- **6 years:** Failure to report a foreign financial account (FBAR) or certain specified foreign financial assets
- **7 years:** If a deduction for worthless securities or bad debts is claimed (IRC Section 6511(d))
- **Unlimited (no expiration):** Fraud or willful attempt to evade tax (IRC Section 6501(c)(1)-(2))
- **Unlimited:** Failure to file a required return (IRC Section 6501(c)(3))
- **3 years from filing of amended return:** For refund claims on amended returns

### Specific Document Types and Minimum Retention
- **Tax returns (filed copies):** Permanently recommended; minimum 7 years
- **Tax workpapers:** 7 years from filing date (covers 6-year substantial understatement period plus buffer)
- **Supporting source documents (receipts, invoices):** 7 years
- **Depreciation schedules and asset records:** Life of asset plus 7 years after disposition
- **Employment tax records (payroll, W-2s, 941s):** 4 years after the later of the due date or date tax was paid (IRC Section 6501(a) plus additional record requirements under Reg. 31.6001-1)
- **Form 5472 and related records:** Permanently recommended (foreign-owned corps, high audit risk)
- **Estimated tax payment records:** 7 years
- **Entity formation documents (articles, EIN letter, bylaws):** Permanently (life of entity plus 7 years after dissolution)
- **Board minutes and resolutions:** Permanently
- **Contracts and agreements:** 7 years after expiration/termination
- **Bank statements and reconciliations:** 7 years
- **General ledger and journals:** 7 years (permanent if feasible)
- **Financial statements:** 7 years minimum; permanent recommended

## State-Specific Requirements

### Florida
- Florida corporate income tax (F-1120) follows federal assessment periods
- Florida Department of Revenue can assess tax within 3 years of the later of: filing date or due date
- Extended to 6 years for substantial understatement
- No statute of limitations for fraud or failure to file
- Florida sales tax records: 3 years from date tax became due or was paid
- Florida Statutes Section 95.091 governs state tax assessment limitations

### General State Guidance
- When operating in multiple states, retain records for the longest applicable period
- Some states have longer assessment periods than the IRS (e.g., California: 4 years general, 8 years for 25% understatement)
- State-specific extensions may apply for amended returns, refund claims, or collection actions

## Client Documents vs. Firm Workpapers

### Client Documents (Originals)
- Original documents provided by the client (bank statements, receipts, contracts) belong to the client
- Return originals promptly after copying or scanning
- If the firm retains originals (with client consent), they must be returned upon request or at engagement termination
- Engagement letter should specify document handling: "Original documents will be returned to the client; the firm will retain copies"

### Firm Workpapers
- Workpapers prepared by the firm in the course of the engagement are the property of the firm
- Includes: trial balances, lead schedules, analytical work, tax computations, review notes, internal memos
- Firm is not required to provide workpapers to the client (unless required by court order or regulatory investigation)
- Workpapers may be provided to a successor accountant with client written consent
- Workpapers must be retained per the firm's retention policy, regardless of client relationship status

### Client-Provided vs. Firm-Generated (Gray Areas)
- Adjusted trial balance prepared from client data: firm workpaper
- Accounting system reports exported by the firm: client data accessed via firm services; provide copies on request
- Tax return copy: client is entitled to a copy; firm retains its own copy
- Engagement letter: both parties should have signed copies
- Correspondence with IRS/state agencies on behalf of client: provide copies to client on request

## Electronic Storage Standards

### Requirements
- Electronic records must be legible, accessible, and reproducible for the full retention period
- Storage media must be durable and protected from degradation (avoid sole reliance on magnetic tape or optical discs without redundancy)
- Maintain backup copies in a separate physical location or cloud storage
- Encrypt electronic records containing PII (see data-security.md)
- Implement access controls: only authorized personnel can access, modify, or delete retained records

### Format Considerations
- PDF/A format recommended for long-term archival (ISO 19005 standard)
- Avoid proprietary formats that may become unreadable as software evolves
- Maintain the ability to search, retrieve, and produce records on demand (indexed storage)
- Cloud storage is acceptable provided the vendor meets security and availability requirements

### IRS Electronic Storage Requirements
- IRS Rev. Proc. 98-25 (as updated): establishes requirements for maintaining books and records in electronic form
- Electronic records must be as accessible as paper originals
- Must be able to produce legible hardcopy on demand
- Index and retrieval system required

## Destruction Procedures

### When to Destroy
- Only after the applicable retention period has expired
- Verify no pending or reasonably anticipated litigation, audit, or investigation before destruction
- Obtain approval from the designated records manager or firm principal
- Document what was destroyed, when, by whom, and the authorization

### How to Destroy
- **Paper records:** Cross-cut shredding or incineration; simple strip shredding is not secure for PII
- **Electronic records:** Secure deletion by overwriting, degaussing, or physical destruction of the storage device
- **Hard drives/SSDs:** Use NIST 800-88 compliant sanitization methods; physical destruction is most certain
- **Cloud-stored records:** Delete from all locations including backups and recycling bins; verify with cloud provider
- Third-party destruction vendors must be vetted (check certifications, e.g., NAID AAA Certified)

### Litigation Hold
- If litigation is pending, threatened, or reasonably anticipated, suspend all destruction of potentially relevant records
- Litigation hold overrides the standard retention schedule
- Notify all relevant personnel of the hold and document it
- Destroying records subject to a litigation hold can result in adverse inference, sanctions, or criminal obstruction charges

## Engagement Letter Retention Clauses

Include the following in every engagement letter:
- "The firm will retain copies of workpapers and filed returns for [7] years from the date of filing"
- "Original client documents will be returned upon request; the firm retains copies"
- "Upon expiration of the retention period, records will be destroyed without further notice"
- "The client is responsible for maintaining their own copies of all tax returns and supporting documents"
- "In the event of firm dissolution, client records will be [transferred to successor firm / returned to client / destroyed per retention schedule]"

## Succession Planning for Client Files

- Firm succession plan must address custody of client records
- Options: transfer to acquiring firm (with client consent), return to clients, retain in escrow with third party
- Notify clients in advance of any firm transition
- Maintain professional liability tail coverage to cover claims arising from transferred engagements
- Successor firm assumes no liability for predecessor work unless explicitly agreed
- Retain a master inventory of all client files, their locations, and their retention expiration dates

## Decision Points

- **7 years vs. permanent:** Permanent retention is cheap with electronic storage. The 7-year minimum covers most risk scenarios, but permanent retention for filed returns and entity documents eliminates any doubt.
- **Paper vs. electronic:** Move to all-electronic storage with paper destruction after scanning and quality verification. Reduces physical storage costs and improves searchability.
- **Cloud vs. local backup:** Cloud primary with local backup redundancy is the recommended approach. Avoid single-point-of-failure storage.
- **Destruction cadence:** Annual review and destruction cycle (e.g., every January, destroy records whose retention period expired in the prior calendar year).
