# Billing and Pricing

**Purpose:** Define pricing models, billing practices, and profitability analysis methods for accounting firm service lines.

**Skills that reference this:** engagement-pricing, client-profitability, billing-management

---

## Pricing Models

### Hourly Billing
- Bill based on actual time spent at a predetermined rate per hour/fraction
- Rate varies by staff level: partner > manager > senior > staff
- Advantages: simple to implement, fair for unpredictable-scope work, easy to justify
- Disadvantages: penalizes efficiency, creates fee uncertainty for clients, encourages scope disputes, caps revenue at hours worked
- Best suited for: first-year engagements with unknown complexity, consulting/advisory with variable scope, IRS audit defense, delinquent filing catch-up projects

### Fixed-Fee Pricing
- Predetermined fee for a defined scope of services
- Fee set based on estimated complexity, prior-year experience, and competitive benchmarks
- Advantages: client fee certainty, rewards firm efficiency, simplifies billing
- Disadvantages: requires accurate scope definition, risk of underpricing complex engagements, scope creep if boundaries are unclear
- Best suited for: recurring tax returns with stable complexity, standard bookkeeping packages, engagements where the firm has multi-year history with the client
- Include a clause in the engagement letter for additional fees if scope changes materially

### Value-Based Pricing
- Fee based on the value delivered to the client rather than cost of production
- Requires understanding the client's perception of value (tax savings, time savings, risk reduction, peace of mind)
- Advantages: decouples revenue from hours, captures premium for expertise, aligns firm and client interests
- Disadvantages: harder to price initially, requires strong client communication about value delivered, may not work for commodity services
- Best suited for: tax planning engagements where specific savings can be quantified, advisory services, complex entity structuring, clients who value speed and certainty over lowest price

### Subscription / Monthly Recurring
- Fixed monthly fee covering a bundled scope of services (e.g., bookkeeping + quarterly financial statements + annual tax return)
- Advantages: predictable revenue for the firm, predictable cost for the client, smooths cash flow seasonality, encourages year-round client engagement
- Disadvantages: requires careful scope definition and utilization monitoring, risk of underpricing if client needs exceed projections
- Best suited for: bookkeeping clients, small business clients wanting comprehensive coverage, advisory retainers
- Structure: define included services clearly, specify exclusions, include annual review/adjustment clause

## Engagement Pricing Factors

### Complexity Drivers
- Entity type: C-corp (Form 1120) > S-corp (Form 1120-S) > sole proprietor (Schedule C) in typical complexity
- Number of states with filing obligations
- Foreign ownership requiring Form 5472
- Multi-year delinquent filings (each year compounds complexity)
- Asset volume and depreciation schedule complexity
- Related-party transactions
- Audit defense risk (aggressive positions require more documentation)
- Industry-specific requirements (real estate, investments, international)

### Risk-Based Pricing Adjustments
- Higher-risk engagements warrant higher fees (more review time, greater liability exposure)
- New clients: premium for first-year setup, learning curve, and uncertainty
- Delinquent filers: premium for penalty/interest calculation, potential IRS correspondence
- Uncooperative clients: premium for additional follow-up and documentation effort
- Rush/deadline-driven work: premium for expedited service

### Timing Considerations
- Returns filed during peak season (Jan-Apr) may command different pricing than off-season
- Extensions add administrative cost (extension filing, two-touch processing)
- Year-end planning engagements priced separately from compliance work

## Financial Metrics

### Realization Rate
- Realization = fees collected / standard fees billed (or, for hourly: collected fees / (hours x standard rate))
- Industry benchmark: 85-95% for well-managed firms
- Below 80% indicates systemic pricing or collection problems
- Track by: engagement type, client, staff member, service line

### Write-Down Analysis
- Write-down = reduction from standard billing to actual billing
- Track write-down reasons: scope underestimate, staff inefficiency, client goodwill adjustment, competitive pressure
- Persistent write-downs for a specific client indicate mispricing -- raise fees or disengage
- Persistent write-downs for a service line indicate systemic pricing or process issues
- Target: write-downs under 10% of total billings

### Client Profitability
- Revenue from client minus direct costs (staff time at cost, software, filing fees) minus allocated overhead
- Rank clients by profitability; the bottom 10-20% typically destroy firm profitability
- Unprofitable clients require: fee increase, scope reduction, process improvement, or disengagement
- Top clients by revenue are not always top clients by profitability (high-revenue clients with heavy write-downs may be unprofitable)

### Effective Hourly Rate
- For fixed-fee engagements: fee collected / actual hours spent
- Compare to the firm's target hourly rate
- If effective rate is consistently below target, the fixed fee is too low or the process is inefficient
- Track over multiple years to identify trends

## Billing Cycle Best Practices

### Invoice Timing
- **Hourly engagements:** Bill monthly or upon engagement completion, whichever is shorter
- **Fixed-fee engagements:** Bill upon delivery (or 50% upfront / 50% on delivery for larger engagements)
- **Subscription:** Bill monthly on a consistent date; auto-payment (ACH/credit card) preferred
- **Retainer-based:** Collect retainer before work begins; replenish when balance falls below threshold

### Invoice Content
- Clear description of services rendered (avoid vague "professional services" line items)
- Reference the engagement letter and agreed fee structure
- Itemize any out-of-scope charges separately with explanation
- Include payment terms, accepted payment methods, and late payment policy

### Collection Procedures
- **30 days past due:** Automated reminder email
- **60 days past due:** Phone call from engagement manager; assess whether to suspend current work
- **90 days past due:** Written demand from firm principal; suspend all non-essential services; consider collections
- **120+ days past due:** Final demand; refer to collections agency or write off; consider disengagement for future services
- Never hold a client's tax return hostage for unpaid fees (potential ethical violation under AICPA Code and state board rules)
- A firm may retain its own workpapers but must provide the client's records upon request per AICPA ET 1.400.200

## Pricing by Service Line

### Tax Preparation (Form 1120)
- Simple (single-state, straightforward income/expense): fixed fee
- Moderate (multi-state, depreciation schedules, officer compensation): fixed fee with complexity tier
- Complex (foreign ownership, Form 5472, multi-year delinquent): hourly or premium fixed fee
- Include/exclude: estimated tax calculations, extension filing, e-filing, IRS correspondence (define in engagement letter)

### Bookkeeping Services
- Monthly transaction-based pricing: define bands (e.g., 0-100 transactions, 101-250, 251+)
- Include: bank reconciliation, financial statement preparation, accounts payable/receivable
- Exclude (price separately): payroll processing, 1099 preparation, sales tax filing, year-end adjustments
- Accounting system subscription cost: client-paid vs. firm-paid (if firm-paid, include in monthly fee with markup)

### Advisory Services
- Value-based pricing for tax planning (tied to projected savings)
- Hourly for ad-hoc consulting
- Retainer for ongoing advisory relationships
- Entity restructuring: project-based fixed fee

## Pricing Review Cadence

- Review pricing annually (align with engagement letter renewal)
- Benchmark against local market rates and national survey data
- Factor in: inflation, staff compensation increases, technology costs, regulatory complexity increases
- Communicate fee changes in the annual engagement letter, not as a surprise on the invoice
- For long-term clients: phase in significant increases over 2-3 years

## Decision Points

- **Hourly vs. fixed for new clients:** Default to hourly for the first year to establish baseline hours, then convert to fixed fee in year two with data-driven pricing.
- **When to fire a client:** When the client is consistently unprofitable after fee adjustment attempts, uncooperative, or creates disproportionate risk.
- **Upfront payment vs. net terms:** For new clients and engagements over a defined threshold, require 50% upfront or full retainer.
- **Bundling discount:** Offering a 5-10% discount for clients who bundle tax + bookkeeping improves retention and smooths revenue.
