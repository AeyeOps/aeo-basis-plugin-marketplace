# Engagement Letters

**Purpose:** Define requirements and best practices for engagement letters across tax preparation, bookkeeping, and advisory service lines.

**Skills that reference this:** client-onboarding, service-line-setup, engagement-management

---

## AICPA Professional Standards

- AICPA Statement on Standards for Tax Services (SSTS) No. 1 requires a CPA to establish a clear understanding with the client regarding the scope of engagement
- AICPA Code of Professional Conduct Rule 1.310.001 (Engagement Letters) establishes documentation requirements
- AU-C Section 210 (Terms of Audit Engagements) provides the model framework adapted for tax and bookkeeping engagements
- Engagement letters are not just best practice -- they are the primary liability shield for the firm

## Required Elements (All Engagement Types)

### Scope of Services
- Enumerate specific services included (e.g., "preparation of federal Form 1120 and Florida Form F-1120 for tax year 2024")
- Explicitly state what is NOT included (e.g., "This engagement does not include bookkeeping, payroll, financial statement preparation, or tax planning advisory services")
- For multi-year engagements, specify each year separately or define the range

### Responsibilities
- **Firm responsibilities:** Prepare returns/deliverables in accordance with applicable professional standards, exercise due professional care, maintain confidentiality
- **Client responsibilities:** Provide complete and accurate information, maintain adequate records, respond to requests timely, review and approve final deliverables before filing
- State that management is responsible for the accuracy and completeness of information provided
- Clarify that the firm will rely on client-provided data without independent verification unless specifically engaged to do so

### Fees and Payment Terms
- Fee structure: fixed fee, hourly rate, or value-based pricing with stated amount or rate
- Billing frequency: upon completion, monthly, milestone-based
- Payment terms: net 30, due upon receipt, retainer requirements
- Late payment consequences: interest charges, suspension of services, withdrawal
- Out-of-scope work: hourly rate for additional services not covered by the engagement
- Include a clause allowing fee adjustment for scope changes or unforeseen complexity

### Limitation of Liability
- Cap firm liability at the amount of fees paid for the engagement (where permitted by state law)
- Include a mutual indemnification clause
- Disclaim liability for penalties and interest arising from client-provided inaccurate information
- Note: Florida law permits limitation of liability clauses in professional services agreements; however, a CPA cannot disclaim liability for gross negligence or willful misconduct

### Dispute Resolution
- Specify mediation as mandatory first step before arbitration or litigation
- Designate governing law (state) and venue (county)
- Include a prevailing-party attorney fees clause
- Consider mandatory binding arbitration clause (reduces litigation cost but waives jury trial right)

### Confidentiality and Data Handling
- Reference IRC Section 7216 restrictions on disclosure of tax return information
- State that client information will be protected per the firm's Written Information Security Plan (WISP)
- Define permitted disclosures: peer review, professional liability insurance, subpoena
- Address electronic transmission security (encrypted email, client portal)

### Term and Termination
- Engagement covers a specific period or tax year
- Either party may terminate with written notice (specify notice period, e.g., 30 days)
- Upon termination, client is responsible for fees for work completed to date
- Firm will return client-provided original documents; firm retains workpapers

## Form 1120 Engagement Specifics

- Specify all forms in scope: Form 1120, Schedule L, Schedule M-1/M-3, Form 5472 (if foreign-owned), state returns
- Note that estimated tax payment calculations are / are not included
- Clarify whether the engagement includes responding to IRS or state notices
- State whether e-filing authorization (Form 8879) is required and how it will be obtained
- Address extension filing: will the firm automatically file Form 7004 if information is not received by a stated deadline?
- For delinquent filings: state the number of years covered and that penalty/interest estimates are advisory only

## Bookkeeping Engagement Specifics

- Define the accounting basis (cash, accrual, or modified accrual)
- Specify deliverables: monthly/quarterly financial statements, bank reconciliations, accounts payable/receivable aging
- Clarify software platform and who owns the subscription
- State that bookkeeping is not an audit, review, or compilation -- no assurance is provided
- Address transaction volume assumptions and additional fees if volume significantly exceeds estimates
- Define document delivery method and deadline for client to provide source documents
- Specify cutoff dates: when monthly books will be closed, when financial statements will be delivered

## Annual Update and Reissuance

- Engagement letters should be reissued or reconfirmed annually, even for recurring engagements
- Material scope changes during the year require an amendment or new letter
- The annual reissuance is an opportunity to update fee schedules, scope, and terms
- Maintain signed copies (physical or electronic with e-signature) in the permanent client file

## Client Acceptance and Continuance Evaluation

### New Client Acceptance
- Verify client identity (government-issued ID for individuals, corporate documents for entities)
- Assess engagement risk: industry, complexity, prior tax compliance history, prior accountant relationship
- Check for conflicts of interest
- Obtain prior-year returns and review for red flags
- If the client was terminated by a prior firm, contact the predecessor (with client consent per AICPA standards)

### Continuance Evaluation (Annual)
- Review fee realization and write-downs for the client
- Assess cooperation level: timeliness of document delivery, responsiveness
- Evaluate risk changes: new IRS audit, litigation involvement, financial distress
- Document the continuance decision

## Disengagement Procedures

- Issue a formal disengagement letter specifying the effective date
- Clearly state which services are ceasing and any remaining obligations (e.g., "We will complete the 2024 Form 1120 but will not prepare the 2025 return")
- Inform the client of upcoming deadlines they must handle with a successor firm
- Return client-provided original documents promptly
- Firm retains workpapers and copies of filed returns per the firm's document retention policy
- Withdraw any pending powers of attorney (IRS Form 2848, CAF withdrawal)
- Send disengagement letter via certified mail or documented electronic delivery

## Decision Points

- **Fixed fee vs. hourly:** Fixed fee for standard, well-defined engagements (Form 1120 with known complexity). Hourly for uncertain scope, first-year engagements, or delinquent filings.
- **Limitation of liability cap:** Set at 1x-3x annual fees depending on risk tolerance and client size.
- **Arbitration vs. litigation:** Arbitration is faster and cheaper but provides limited appeal rights.
- **Bundled vs. separate letters:** Separate engagement letters for tax and bookkeeping services, even for the same client, to clearly delineate scope and liability.
