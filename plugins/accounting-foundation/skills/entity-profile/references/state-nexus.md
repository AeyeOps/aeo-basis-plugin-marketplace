# State Income Tax Nexus: Corporate Filing Obligations

**Purpose**: Determine which states a corporation must file income tax returns in, with deep focus on Florida corporate income tax rules.

**Skills**: —

---

## Nexus Fundamentals

**Nexus** is the minimum connection between a taxpayer and a state that gives the state authority to impose tax. Two types matter for income tax:

### Physical Presence Nexus (Traditional)

- Employees working in the state
- Owned or leased property in the state
- Inventory stored in the state (including FBA warehouses)
- Independent contractors performing services in the state (some states)
- Regular solicitation visits by company representatives

### Economic Nexus / Factor Presence

Post-Wayfair (2018), states increasingly apply economic nexus to income tax (not just sales tax). The Multistate Tax Commission (MTC) factor presence standard:

- **$500,000+ in property** in the state, OR
- **$500,000+ in payroll** in the state, OR
- **$500,000+ in sales** sourced to the state, OR
- **25% of total property, payroll, or sales** in the state

States adopting some form of economic nexus for income tax: CA, CO, CT, HI, MA, MI, NY, OH, OR, PA, WA, and others. Each state sets its own thresholds.

## P.L. 86-272 Protection

Federal law (15 USC 381-384) prohibits states from imposing **net income tax** on corporations whose only in-state activity is solicitation of orders for sales of **tangible personal property**, where orders are:

- Sent outside the state for approval, AND
- Filled and shipped from outside the state

### What P.L. 86-272 Protects

- Soliciting orders in person, by phone, or by mail for tangible personal property
- Activities ancillary to solicitation (advertising, carrying samples)

### What P.L. 86-272 Does NOT Protect

- Sales of services (no protection)
- Sales of intangibles (no protection)
- Leasing tangible personal property (no protection)
- Maintaining an office or warehouse in the state
- Approving or filling orders in the state
- Collecting delinquent accounts in the state
- **Post-2021**: Many states now assert that internet-based activities (website cookies, app-based interactions, marketplace facilitation) exceed P.L. 86-272 protection. MTC issued revised guidance in 2021 treating extensive web-based interactivity as exceeding solicitation

### P.L. 86-272 Relevance

If the client entity sells only services (consulting, technology), P.L. 86-272 provides zero protection. Nexus is determined solely by physical presence and factor presence tests.

## Florida Corporate Income Tax

### Who Must File

Every corporation (including S-corps that are subject to federal income tax, foreign corporations with FL income, and corporations doing business in FL) that has nexus with Florida and has federal taxable income (or any Florida additions) exceeding $50,000.

### Tax Rate and Exemption

- **Rate** — 5.5% of Florida taxable income
- **Exemption** — $50,000 (not a deduction; Florida taxable income up to $50,000 is exempt)
- **No personal income tax** — Florida taxes only corporate income, not individual income

### Computing Florida Taxable Income

1. Start with **federal taxable income** (Line 30, Form 1120, before NOL and special deductions)
2. Add **Florida additions** — includes Florida tax-exempt interest, certain intangible expenses paid to related parties
3. Subtract **Florida subtractions** — includes Florida NOL deduction, certain dividend adjustments
4. Apply **apportionment factor** (if multistate)
5. Result is **Florida taxable income** subject to 5.5% rate (minus $50,000 exemption)

### Apportionment Formula

Florida uses a **single sales factor** for apportionment (effective for tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2019). Before 2019, Florida used a double-weighted sales factor in a three-factor formula.

Sales factor: Florida sales / Total sales everywhere

Market-based sourcing for services: Revenue sourced to the state where the benefit of the service is received.

### Florida NOL Rules

- Florida generally conforms to federal NOL rules but maintains a **separate Florida NOL carryforward**
- Florida NOL = the apportioned loss in a given year
- Florida adopted the TCJA 80% limitation for post-2017 NOLs
- Florida did NOT conform to the CARES Act 5-year carryback for 2018-2020 NOLs
- Pre-2018 Florida NOLs: 20-year carryforward (federal was also 20 years pre-TCJA)
- Post-2017 Florida NOLs: Indefinite carryforward (conforming to federal)

### Florida Estimated Tax

- Required if expected FL tax liability exceeds $2,500
- Due dates align with federal estimated payment schedule
- Underpayment penalty: 12% per annum on the underpayment amount

### Florida Filing Deadlines

- Due date: Same as federal (April 15 for calendar-year corps)
- Extension: Automatic if federal extension (Form 7004) is filed; extends through federal extended due date
- Must attach copy of federal extension to Florida return
- Late filing penalty: 10% per month, up to 50% of tax due

### Florida Conformity to IRC

Florida piggybacks on the Internal Revenue Code as of **January 1 of the current tax year** (rolling conformity). The legislature periodically updates the conformity date. Key non-conformities:

- Florida does not conform to CARES Act NOL carryback provisions
- Florida has its own addback rules for related-party intangible expenses
- Florida bonus depreciation conformity has varied — check the year-specific conformity date

| Tax Year | Rate | Exemption | Apportionment Formula | NOL Rules | IRC Conformity Date |
|----------|------|-----------|----------------------|-----------|-------------------|
| 2007-2011 | 5.5% | $5,000 | 3-factor, double-weighted sales | 20-year carryforward, 2-year carryback | Rolling (Jan 1 of tax year) |
| 2012-2018 | 5.5% | $50,000 | 3-factor, double-weighted sales (pre-2019) | 20-year carryforward, no carryback (post-2017) | Rolling |
| 2019 | 5.5% | $50,000 | **Single sales factor** (new) | 80% limit applies (post-2017 NOLs) | Rolling |
| 2020 | 5.5% | $50,000 | Single sales factor | 80% limit; no CARES Act carryback | Rolling |
| 2021 | 3.535% | $50,000 | Single sales factor | Same as 2020 | Rolling |
| 2022 | 5.5% | $50,000 | Single sales factor | Same | Rolling |
| 2023-2024 | 5.5% | $50,000 | Single sales factor | Same | Rolling |
| 2025-2026 | 5.5% | $50,000 | Single sales factor | Same | Rolling (check legislature) |

**Note on 2021 rate**: Florida temporarily reduced the corporate income tax rate to 3.535% for tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2021 and before January 1, 2022 (HB 7061). The rate reverted to 5.5% for 2022.

**Note on exemption**: The $50,000 exemption was increased from $5,000 effective for tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2012 (HB 7087).

## Multistate Filing Considerations

- **Combined reporting**: Florida does NOT require combined/unitary reporting. Each corporation files separately unless electing consolidated
- **Throwback rule**: Florida does NOT have a throwback rule (sales not taxed elsewhere are excluded from the FL numerator)
- **PL 86-272**: Florida respects P.L. 86-272 for tangible personal property sales
- **Voluntary disclosure**: FL DOR has a voluntary disclosure program for corporations that should have been filing but were not — reduces lookback to 3 years and waives penalties
