## 1. Overview of QBO Chart of Accounts

QuickBooks Online automatically creates a chart of accounts (COA) when you set up your company, based on your business entity type and industry selection. The COA organizes accounts into types and detail types that determine where transactions appear on financial reports.

**Source:** [Learn about the chart of accounts in QuickBooks Online](https://quickbooks.intuit.com/learn-support/en-us/help-article/chart-accounts/learn-chart-accounts-quickbooks-online/L2yc6KBob_US_en_US)

## 2. Account Types and Detail Types

### Structure

QBO accounts have two classification levels:

- **Account Type** — the top-level category (Bank, Accounts Receivable, Expense, etc.). Determines which financial statement the account appears on.
- **Detail Type** — a subcategory within the account type. Provides more specificity for reporting purposes. According to Intuit: "Detail types don't have any impact on your financials, but they give more information about what you can track with each account type."

There are 15 account types and approximately 154 detail types in QBO.

## 3. Default Accounts vs. Custom Accounts

### How Default Accounts Are Created

When you create a company in QBO, specific default accounts are automatically created based on the business entity type selected. Additional special accounts are created when certain features are turned on (e.g., enabling inventory creates Inventory Asset and Cost of Goods Sold accounts).

### Official Guidance: Use Defaults Where Possible

Intuit's official guidance recommends using the default accounts as a foundation and customizing by:
- Renaming default accounts to match your terminology
- Adding custom sub-accounts under appropriate parent types
- Making unused default accounts inactive (not deleting them)

Renaming is preferred over creating parallel accounts because it preserves account integrity without fragmenting transaction history.

### Importing vs. Manual Creation

For new companies, you can either import a COA via CSV or manually create accounts. Intuit recommends importing when migrating from another system to maintain structure, but manual creation gives more control for greenfield setups.

**Sources:**
- [Manage default and special accounts in your QuickBooks Online chart of accounts](https://quickbooks.intuit.com/learn-support/en-us/help-article/chart-accounts/manage-default-special-accounts-chart-accounts/L3WvLaIfa_US_en_US)

## 5. Accounts Receivable (A/R)

### Rules

- QBO automatically creates one A/R account. Most businesses need only one.
- The account type is "Accounts Receivable (A/R)" — this account type is reserved for QBO's receivables system.
- **You cannot use more than one A/R account in the same transaction.** (Official warning message in QBO.)
- The default A/R account cannot be deleted if it has transactions.
- You can rename it (e.g., from "Accounts Receivable (A/R)" to "Accounts Receivable").
- You can create additional A/R accounts, but this is not recommended as it complicates reporting.

### Best Practice

Use a single A/R account. If multiple A/R accounts exist from a prior migration or setup error, merge them (note: merging cannot be undone).

## 7. Opening Balance Equity

### Purpose

Opening Balance Equity (OBE) is an auto-created temporary equity account. QBO uses it as the offsetting entry when you enter beginning balances for accounts during initial setup or account creation. Since double-entry accounting requires balanced entries, QBO credits/debits OBE as a placeholder.

Example: Setting up a bank account with a $10,000 opening balance creates:
- Debit: Bank account $10,000
- Credit: Opening Balance Equity $10,000

### Target State

**OBE should have a $0.00 balance in steady-state operations.** A non-zero OBE balance indicates unresolved setup entries.

### How to Zero It Out

After all opening balances are entered, create a journal entry to transfer OBE to the appropriate equity account:

- For a C Corporation: Debit OBE, Credit Paid-In Capital or Retained Earnings
- For sole proprietor/S Corp: Debit OBE, Credit Owner's Equity

### Important Rules

- Cannot be deleted or made inactive.
- You can edit the name if needed.
- After initial setup is complete, OBE should never receive new entries in normal operations.
- Once zeroed out, Intuit recommends marking the account inactive (though QBO may prevent this).

## 8. Retained Earnings

### How QBO Handles Retained Earnings

QBO **automatically calculates and posts Retained Earnings** — there is no year-end close process in QBO. The mechanism works as follows:

- During the fiscal year, net income flows into the Profit & Loss section of the Balance Sheet as "Net Income."
- On the first day of the new fiscal year, QBO automatically reclassifies the prior year's Net Income as Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet.
- This is a **report-time calculation only** — QBO does not create an actual journal entry or "close" the books. The shift appears when you run reports dated after the fiscal year-end.

Example: Fiscal year ending Dec 31.
- Balance sheet dated Dec 31: shows Net Income in equity section
- Balance sheet dated Jan 1: shows same amount as Retained Earnings

### Rules

- Cannot be deleted — it is a special system account.
- Cannot be made inactive — it is a special type of Balance Sheet account with this restriction.
- **Cannot stop the automatic year-end update** — QBO is designed to perform this calculation and there is no setting to disable it.
- You can edit the account name if necessary.
- You can see a year-by-year breakdown by running a Balance Sheet by Year report.

### Manual Entries to Retained Earnings

If you need to record prior-year retained earnings for historical reconstruction, you can post journal entries directly to Retained Earnings. This is the correct approach when entering beginning balances for a company that was operating before being set up in QBO.

## 9. Undeposited Funds

### Purpose

Undeposited Funds is a special holding account (type: Other Current Assets) that tracks customer payments received but not yet deposited to a bank account. It functions as a clearing account — the real-world equivalent of checks sitting in a drawer before being taken to the bank.

When a customer payment is received in QBO, it defaults to Undeposited Funds. When you record the actual bank deposit, QBO moves the amount from Undeposited Funds to the bank account.

### Rules

- Cannot be deleted, deactivated, or made inactive — it is a permanent system account.
- Cannot be reconciled (it is not a bank account).
- Should be monitored for stale items — old amounts sitting in Undeposited Funds indicate payments recorded but never deposited in QBO.

## 10. Unapplied Cash Payment Income and Unapplied Cash Bill Payment Expense

### Purpose

These are system accounts created by QBO for cash-basis reporting. They appear on cash-basis Profit & Loss reports when:

- **Unapplied Cash Payment Income**: A customer payment is received before the corresponding invoice is dated (payment date precedes invoice date). On cash basis, the income is recognized when cash is received.
- **Unapplied Cash Bill Payment Expense**: A vendor payment is sent before the corresponding bill is dated. On cash basis, the expense is recognized when cash is paid.

These accounts automatically reverse themselves once the payment is matched to the invoice/bill.

### Rules

- Cannot be deleted or changed — created for cash-basis reporting and are permanent system accounts.
- If you see these on reports in accrual basis, it indicates a data entry timing issue (payment dated before invoice).

## 11. Renaming vs. Creating New Accounts

### Official Guidance

Intuit's guidance favors **renaming existing accounts over creating new ones** in most cases. Key reasons:

1. **Renaming preserves history** — all existing transactions remain associated with the account.
2. **Creating a new account creates a parallel account** — historical transactions stay on the old account, and you must remember to use the new one going forward.
3. **The 250-account limit** (see Section 13) makes unnecessary account creation wasteful.

### When Renaming Is Appropriate

- Renaming a default account to match your preferred terminology (e.g., "Services" → "Consulting Revenue")
- Renaming an account to better reflect its actual use
- All default accounts except the true system accounts (Retained Earnings, Opening Balance Equity, etc.) can have their names edited

### Warning: Renaming to Merge

If you rename an account to exactly match another existing account's name, QBO will prompt you to merge the two accounts. **Merging cannot be undone.** All transactions from both accounts will combine into one account.

### When Creating New Accounts Is Appropriate

- When you need a categorization that genuinely does not exist in the default COA
- When adding sub-accounts under a parent for more detailed tracking
- When your business has distinct revenue streams or expense categories that warrant separate tracking

### Caution on Renaming to Stay Under Account Limits

Intuit explicitly warns: "if you are thinking of renaming accounts to stay under the limits, I want to caution you that you will lose the historical data on the original account." This implies renaming for consolidation purposes can cause loss of meaningful historical categorization.

## 12. Naming Conventions

Intuit's guidance on account naming is minimal but practical:

- Use names that are meaningful to you and your accountant
- Use straightforward, unambiguous titles (e.g., "Bank Charges," "Office Supplies," "Consulting Revenue")
- Account names must be unique within QBO — you cannot have two accounts with the same name
- For sub-accounts, names do not need to be globally unique if they are under different parent accounts (though QBO may enforce uniqueness across all accounts in some versions)

### Account Numbers

Account numbers are optional in QBO and must be enabled manually:
1. Go to Gear icon → Account and Settings → Advanced
2. Enable "Account numbers" in the Chart of Accounts section
3. Optionally enable "Show account numbers" on reports and transactions

Account numbers are not assigned automatically — you must enter them manually. They can be 4-5 digits or include letters. Standard industry conventions:
- 1000-1999: Assets
- 2000-2999: Liabilities
- 3000-3999: Equity
- 4000-4999: Income
- 5000-5999: Cost of Goods Sold
- 6000-7999: Expenses
- 8000-8999: Other Income
- 9000-9999: Other Expense

## 13. Account Limits

### Per-Plan Limits

| QBO Plan | Chart of Accounts Limit |
|----------|------------------------|
| Simple Start | 250 accounts |
| Essentials | 250 accounts |
| Plus | 250 accounts |
| Advanced | Unlimited |

### What Counts Toward the Limit

- All active accounts created by the user count toward the limit.
- Sub-accounts count the same as parent accounts.
- **System/default accounts (Undeposited Funds, Opening Balance Equity, Retained Earnings, etc.) do NOT count toward the user limit.**
- Inactive accounts do NOT count toward the limit.

### Sub-Account Structure

- Maximum depth: 4 levels below root (5 total levels including parent)
- No limit on the number of sub-accounts under a single parent
- Sub-accounts inherit their parent's account type

## 14. Tax Mapping Implications of Account Type Choices

### Critical Finding: Tax-Line Mapping Is NOT Available in QBO

**Direct tax-line mapping (assigning an account to a specific IRS form line) is only available in QuickBooks Desktop, not QuickBooks Online.**

In QBO, the "tax form" setting under Company Information (Settings → Account and Settings → Company → Company type → Tax form) is primarily an integration setting used when exporting data to Intuit's tax preparation software (TurboTax Business, ProConnect). It does not create functional tax-line mappings within QBO itself.

Key implications:
- QBO cannot produce Form 1120 (C Corp), Form 1120S (S Corp), or Form 1065 (Partnership) directly.
- For C Corporations: TurboTax Business or ProConnect Tax are required to prepare the actual return from QBO data.
- The "Tax form" setting (e.g., selecting Form 1120) primarily affects how accounts map when data is exported to those tax products.

### How Detail Types Relate to Tax Reporting

While QBO does not provide direct tax-line mapping, account detail types do influence how transactions are categorized when exported to Intuit tax software. Examples:
- Advertising/Promotional detail type → Line 8 of Schedule C (for sole proprietors)
- Compensation of Officers detail type → Maps to officer compensation lines on 1120/1120S

Intuit's help article "Learn where detail types appear on your tax forms" documents these mappings, but the feature primarily affects Schedule C (Form 1040) users, not corporate returns.

### Changing the Tax Form Setting

Warning from Intuit: "Changing the tax form will result in currently mapped accounts to be unmapped." If you change the entity type setting, any existing account-to-tax-line associations are cleared.

This setting **does not alter your financial data** — only the mapping metadata.

### Best Practice for C Corporations

For a C Corporation using QBO:
1. Set Company type → Tax form to "Corporation (Form 1120)"
2. Use appropriate detail types on all accounts (they provide semantic meaning even without direct line mapping)
3. Use your external tax preparer's software (or ProConnect/TurboTax Business) for the actual Form 1120 preparation
4. Ensure your COA structure aligns with Form 1120 line items to facilitate year-end tax work

## 15. Best Practices Summary

### Account Organization

- Create separate accounts for distinct income types rather than lumping all income into one account — some income streams are cheap to generate while others are costly, and separate tracking enables profitability analysis.
- Break expenses into meaningful sub-categories rather than broad buckets.
- Use sub-accounts under parent accounts to achieve detail without multiplying top-level accounts.
- Keep parent accounts at the summary level; post transactions to sub-accounts.

### Account Lifecycle

- **Add accounts as new needs arise** — do not pre-create speculative accounts.
- **Wait until year-end or quarter-end to inactivate old accounts** — removing accounts mid-period can complicate reconciliations.
- **Make unused accounts inactive rather than deleting** — inactive accounts don't count toward the 250-account limit and can be reactivated if needed.
- **Do not delete accounts with transaction history** — transaction history is lost if an account is merged away, and some accounts cannot be deleted at all.

### Setup and Migration

- Run a Profit and Loss report covering 2-3 years to identify which accounts are actively used before making structural changes.
- Sort COA by balance to identify zero-balance accounts eligible for inactivation.
- Check for duplicate accounts (same purpose, different name) and merge them.
- Consider whether accounts would be better structured as sub-accounts under a parent rather than as standalone top-level accounts.

### Special Account Handling

- Never post regular transactions to Opening Balance Equity — it is for initial setup only.
- Monitor Undeposited Funds for stale items indicating incomplete deposit recording.
- Do not attempt to delete or work around system accounts (Retained Earnings, Unapplied Cash accounts, etc.) — QBO will recreate them or prevent the action.
- If Sales of Product Income appears unwanted, be aware that renaming or deleting it will cause QBO to recreate it as long as inventory/product features are enabled.

### For Historical Reconstruction (Retroactive Setup)

When entering historical data for prior years:
- Post opening balances for the earliest year's balance sheet to Opening Balance Equity, then journal entry to proper equity accounts.
- Post retained earnings for intervening years directly to the Retained Earnings account via journal entries.
- QBO's automatic Retained Earnings calculation will not retroactively reconstruct prior years — you must enter those balances manually.
- Use dated journal entries (dated within the relevant fiscal year) so QBO's year-by-year reports are accurate.

## 16. Entity Type and COA Implications for C Corporations

When QBO is set up for a C Corporation, the following equity accounts are relevant:

- **Common Stock** — paid-in capital from stock issuance
- **Preferred Stock** — if applicable
- **Paid-In Capital or Surplus** — additional paid-in capital above par value
- **Retained Earnings** — accumulated earnings (auto-managed by QBO)
- **Treasury Stock** — stock repurchased by the corporation
- **Dividends Paid** — shareholder distributions

The **Accumulated Adjustment** account is specific to S Corporations (tracks the accumulated adjustments account on Form 1120S Schedule M-2) and should not be used for a C Corporation.

For Form 1120 Schedule M-2 (Analysis of Unappropriated Retained Earnings), the Retained Earnings account in QBO directly corresponds to the Schedule M-2 ending balance.

## 17. Limitations and Known Issues

- **QBO does not produce tax returns** — it is bookkeeping software, not tax preparation software. Form 1120 must be prepared externally.
- **No direct tax-line mapping in QBO** (see Section 14).
- **250-account limit** for Simple Start, Essentials, and Plus plans.
- **4-level sub-account depth limit.**
- **Inventory accounts (Inventory Asset, COGS, Sales of Product Income) cannot be removed** even for service businesses — if inventory tracking is enabled, these accounts persist.
- **System accounts auto-recreate** — attempting to delete Sales of Product Income, Services, or similar accounts results in QBO recreating them, sometimes with "-1" appended to the name.
- **Retained Earnings cannot be manually closed** — QBO calculates this at report time; there is no year-end close transaction.
- **The "Tax form" setting is largely cosmetic in QBO** — it has no functional impact unless you export to Intuit tax software.
