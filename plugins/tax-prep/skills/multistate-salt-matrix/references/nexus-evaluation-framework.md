# Nexus Evaluation Framework: Multistate Corporate Income Tax

**Purpose**: Cross-state nexus evaluation matrix for C-corporations operating in multiple states — physical presence, economic nexus, factor presence, affiliate/attributional nexus, P.L. 86-272 protection analysis, and voluntary disclosure programs.

**Skills**: multistate-salt-matrix

---

## Nexus Type Taxonomy

Five distinct nexus theories can independently create a state income tax filing obligation. A corporation must evaluate each theory for every state where it has activity.

### 1. Physical Presence Nexus

The traditional Constitutional standard (pre-Wayfair): a corporation has nexus in any state where it maintains a physical footprint.

**Triggering facts (any one is sufficient)**:
- Employees performing services in-state (including remote workers whose home office is in-state)
- Owned or leased real property (offices, warehouses, retail locations)
- Owned or leased tangible personal property (equipment, inventory, vehicles)
- Inventory stored in-state (including consignment inventory at third-party locations and marketplace fulfillment warehouses)
- Contractor activity — some states attribute independent contractor presence to the hiring company when the contractor performs services on the company's behalf
- Mobile workforce — states with mobile workforce rules may assert nexus when employees enter the state beyond a de minimis threshold (day-count varies by state)

**Remote worker complications**: Post-2020, remote employees increasingly trigger nexus in their home states. States differ on whether a single remote employee creates nexus or whether de minimis thresholds apply. Convenience-of-the-employer rules (notably NY and some followers) may source the employee's income to the employer's state rather than the remote worker's state.

### 2. Economic Nexus (Income Tax)

Post-South Dakota v. Wayfair (2018), the Supreme Court held that physical presence is not required for nexus under the Commerce Clause, replacing it with a substantial economic presence test. Although Wayfair addressed sales tax, states rapidly extended the principle to corporate income tax.

**State approaches**:
- Some states enacted new economic nexus statutes citing Wayfair
- Others rely on pre-existing factor presence statutes that already imposed nexus without physical presence
- A minority still formally require physical presence for income tax (though this is shrinking)

The key distinction from sales tax economic nexus: income tax economic nexus thresholds are typically based on property, payroll, and sales attributed to the state — not gross receipts alone.

### 3. Factor Presence Nexus (MTC Model)

The Multistate Tax Commission (MTC) adopted a model factor presence nexus statute that creates income tax nexus when a corporation exceeds any single threshold:

- Property in-state (owned and rented, at original cost)
- Payroll in-state (compensation to employees working in-state)
- Sales sourced to the state under that state's sourcing rules
- A percentage of total property, payroll, or sales attributable to the state

States that adopt the MTC model set their own dollar amounts and percentages. The structural framework is consistent but the specific thresholds are state-determined and may be adjusted periodically.

**Interaction with economic nexus**: Factor presence is one form of economic nexus. States may use the MTC model exactly, adapt it with different thresholds, or implement their own economic nexus statute that differs structurally.

### 4. Affiliate / Attributional Nexus

Some states attribute the nexus-creating activities of one entity to a related entity, even if the related entity has no independent nexus.

**Common theories**:
- **Agency nexus** — an in-state entity acts as the agent of the out-of-state entity (performing services, soliciting sales, maintaining inventory on behalf of the out-of-state company)
- **Unitary business nexus** — when entities are part of a unitary business, the in-state entity's presence may create nexus for the entire unitary group in combined reporting states
- **Affiliate nexus statutes** — some states explicitly provide that an out-of-state entity has nexus if an in-state affiliate uses substantially similar trademarks, service marks, or trade names

**Holding company structures**: States with addback statutes or combined reporting may attribute nexus to an out-of-state holding company that receives royalties or interest from an in-state operating subsidiary.

### 5. Statutory / Doing Business Nexus

Some states define nexus by statute as "doing business" in the state, which may be interpreted more broadly than physical presence or factor presence. Activities such as owning intangible property used in-state, licensing intellectual property, or having customers in-state may trigger "doing business" nexus in certain jurisdictions.

## P.L. 86-272 Protection Analysis

Public Law 86-272 (15 USC 381-384) is a federal statute that prohibits states from imposing net income tax on corporations whose only in-state activity is solicitation of orders for tangible personal property.

### Statutory Requirements (All Must Be Met)

1. The corporation sells **tangible personal property** (not services, not intangibles, not leased property)
2. In-state activities are limited to **solicitation of orders**
3. Orders are **sent outside the state** for approval or rejection
4. Orders are **filled from outside the state** (shipped from an out-of-state location)

### Protected Activities (Within Solicitation)

Activities treated as ancillary to solicitation and therefore protected:
- In-person, telephone, or mail solicitation by company employees
- Advertising and promotional activities
- Carrying product samples for display (not for sale or distribution)
- Soliciting and accepting returns/exchanges within the company's return policy
- Coordinating shipment of replacement products for defective goods
- Missionary sales activities (product demonstration, technical assistance) when no separate charge and subordinate to solicitation

### Unprotected Activities (Exceeding Solicitation)

Activities that independently create nexus and destroy P.L. 86-272 protection:
- Approving or rejecting orders in-state
- Filling orders from in-state inventory
- Making repairs or providing maintenance
- Collecting delinquent accounts through in-state visits
- Investigating creditworthiness
- Installing or supervising installation
- Conducting training after the sale (not ancillary)
- Owning or leasing real property
- Maintaining a sample or display room for more than 14 days in any tax year
- Carrying inventory for sale or distribution (beyond samples)

### Internet Activity and P.L. 86-272 (MTC 2021 Revised Statement)

In August 2021, the MTC adopted a revised statement on P.L. 86-272 addressing internet-era business activities. Key positions:

**Not protected under the MTC statement**:
- Selling products or services through a website or app to in-state customers
- Streaming or providing digital content (video, music, applications) to in-state users
- Using cookies or other tracking technology to gather in-state consumer data for purposes beyond solicitation (behavioral advertising, personalization)
- Providing post-sale assistance (chat, help desk) through electronic means to in-state customers
- Offering and managing reward/loyalty programs through internet or app for in-state customers
- Contracting with in-state content creators or influencers

**Still protected under the MTC statement**:
- Displaying static product information on a website accessible to in-state customers
- Allowing customers to search for product information, including availability
- Placing internet cookies on in-state devices when used solely for internal purposes (website analytics, not consumer-specific use)
- Sending email/electronic advertising to in-state customers

**State adoption**: The MTC statement is a model/recommendation — each state decides independently whether and how to adopt it. As of the verification date, multiple states have formally adopted or indicated they will follow the MTC revised statement. Others have not addressed it. The trend is toward broader adoption.

### P.L. 86-272 Decision Tree

1. Does the corporation sell tangible personal property? If NO → no protection (P.L. 86-272 irrelevant); proceed to nexus analysis without it
2. Does the corporation also sell services or intangibles in-state? If YES → P.L. 86-272 does not protect the service/intangible revenue stream; separate analysis required
3. Are all in-state activities limited to solicitation (including ancillary activities)? If NO → protection lost; corporation has nexus
4. Are orders approved and filled outside the state? If NO → protection lost
5. Does the corporation engage in internet activities exceeding the protected list? Check the specific state's position on the MTC 2021 statement
6. Result: P.L. 86-272 applies only if all conditions in steps 1-5 are satisfied

## Voluntary Disclosure Agreements (VDA)

A voluntary disclosure agreement is a negotiated resolution between a taxpayer and a state when the taxpayer has unfiled returns in that state. Most states offer VDA programs, either directly through the state revenue department or through the MTC's National Nexus Program.

### Structure of a Typical VDA

- **Lookback period**: States typically limit the lookback to 3-4 years (compared to a full statute of limitations if discovered by audit, which may be indefinite for non-filers)
- **Penalty waiver**: Most states waive all penalties as part of the VDA agreement
- **Interest**: Generally not waived — interest accrues on the unpaid tax for the lookback period
- **Tax payment**: Full tax liability for the lookback period must be paid (installment arrangements may be negotiated)
- **Future compliance**: The taxpayer must commit to filing and paying in all prospective years

### MTC National Nexus Program

The MTC operates a multistate VDA program that allows a corporation to simultaneously negotiate with multiple states through a single point of contact. Benefits:
- Standardized process across participating states
- Confidentiality — the corporation's identity is not disclosed to the state until a tentative agreement is reached
- Uniform lookback periods for participating states

Not all states participate. Some states (notably CA) do not participate in the MTC program and must be approached directly.

### VDA Negotiation Considerations

- **Pre-disclosure planning**: Estimate tax liability in each state before initiating disclosure. Some states may have minimal liability, making voluntary disclosure unnecessary
- **Statute of limitations risk**: Non-filers generally have no statute of limitations running. The VDA sets an artificial lookback period that substitutes for the open-ended exposure
- **Nexus triggering event**: States may ask what activity created nexus. Be prepared to identify the nexus-triggering activity and its start date
- **Audit protection**: VDA agreements typically protect only the pre-lookback period. The lookback period itself and future years remain subject to normal audit
- **Timing**: Initiate VDA before the state discovers the non-filing. If the state contacts the taxpayer first (letter, notice, or audit), VDA eligibility may be foreclosed

### State-by-State VDA Availability

Virtually all states with an income tax offer some form of voluntary disclosure program. However:
- Some states require VDA application through the MTC program only
- Some states accept direct applications but not MTC applications
- Some states accept both
- Lookback periods, while typically 3-4 years, vary. A few states impose longer lookbacks
- Certain states have permanent VDA programs; others open windows periodically

## Nexus Evaluation Workflow (Multistate)

For a corporation operating in multiple states, the nexus evaluation should proceed systematically:

1. **Inventory all state contacts**: employees (including remote), property, sales, contractors, affiliated entities, internet activities — list every state
2. **Apply physical presence test**: for each state, determine if any physical presence fact exists
3. **Apply economic nexus / factor presence test**: for states without physical presence, check whether the state has an economic nexus or factor presence statute and whether the corporation exceeds the thresholds
4. **Check affiliate / attributional nexus**: for states where the corporation has no direct presence, determine if an affiliate's activities create attributed nexus
5. **Apply P.L. 86-272 filter**: for states where nexus exists (from steps 2-4), determine if P.L. 86-272 protects the corporation from income tax in that state
6. **Assess internet activity impact**: for states that adopted the MTC 2021 revised statement, evaluate whether the corporation's internet activities exceed the P.L. 86-272 safe harbor
7. **Result**: filing obligation list — states where the corporation must file an income tax return
8. **Evaluate VDA opportunity**: for any state where the corporation has nexus but has not been filing, assess voluntary disclosure program eligibility and lookback exposure
