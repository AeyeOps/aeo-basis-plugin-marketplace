# Form 3115 — Application for Change in Accounting Method

**Purpose**: Filing mechanics for Form 3115 under automatic and non-automatic consent procedures, including duplicate filing requirements, signature rules, window period restrictions, eligibility criteria, and audit protection under Rev. Proc. 2015-13.

**Skills**: `tx/accounting-method-changes`

---

## Overview of Accounting Method Changes

IRC 446(e) requires IRS consent for any change in accounting method. A "method of accounting" involves the timing of when income or expenses are recognized — not corrections of mathematical or posting errors, which are adjustments to specific items. The distinction is critical: a method change requires Form 3115 and produces a 481(a) adjustment; an error correction is reported on an amended return or current-year adjustment.

Two consent paths exist:

- **Automatic consent** — Filed under Rev. Proc. 2015-13 (as modified by Rev. Proc. 2024-23) for changes listed in the automatic change procedures. No IRS review or user fee required.
- **Non-automatic (advance) consent** — Filed as a ruling request to the IRS National Office under Rev. Proc. 2015-13 Section 9. Requires user fee payment and IRS review.

## Form 3115 Filing Mechanics

### Who Files

The applicant (taxpayer making the method change) files Form 3115. For consolidated groups, the common parent files on behalf of the member. For partnerships and S corporations, the entity files — not individual partners/shareholders.

### When to File — Automatic Consent

Under Rev. Proc. 2015-13 Section 6.03(1):

- File the original Form 3115 with the federal income tax return (including extensions) for the **year of change** — the first year the new method is used
- File a **duplicate copy** with the IRS National Office in Ogden, UT (or as specified in current procedures) no earlier than the first day of the year of change and no later than the date the original is filed with the return
- The duplicate filing requirement is mandatory — failure to timely file the duplicate does not invalidate the change if the original was timely filed with the return, but the IRS may treat it as a procedural deficiency

### When to File — Non-Automatic (Advance) Consent

Under Rev. Proc. 2015-13 Section 9:

- File during the **tax year** for which the change is requested (the year of change)
- The application must be filed during the year of change — not with the return for that year
- Pay the applicable IRS user fee with the submission (currently $12,600 for most non-automatic changes; reduced rates for small businesses)

### Signature Requirements

- The taxpayer or authorized representative must sign the form
- For corporations: an officer with authority to bind the entity (generally an officer listed on the return)
- A power of attorney (Form 2848) is required if a representative signs
- For consolidated groups: the common parent signs

### Required Attachments and Schedules

Form 3115 includes four parts and multiple schedules:

- **Part I** — Information for automatic change request (identification, DCN, year of change)
- **Part II** — Information for non-automatic change request (additional detail, legal analysis)
- **Part III** — Method change information (present method, proposed method, justification)
- **Part IV** — Section 481(a) adjustment (computation, spread period election)
- **Schedule A** — Change in overall method (cash to accrual or vice versa)
- **Schedule B** — Change in depreciation or amortization
- **Schedule C** — Change in method for long-term contracts
- **Schedule D** — Change related to UNICAP (Section 263A)
- **Schedule E** — Change in inventory method

Only the applicable schedules need to be completed. Attach supporting computations for the 481(a) adjustment.

## Automatic Consent Procedures — Rev. Proc. 2015-13

### Scope

Rev. Proc. 2015-13 (as modified by Rev. Proc. 2024-23 and other subsequent modifications) provides the exclusive procedures for automatic consent method changes. The specific changes eligible for automatic treatment are listed in an annually updated appendix (most recently Rev. Proc. 2024-23).

### Eligibility Requirements (Section 5.01)

The taxpayer is eligible for automatic consent if ALL of the following are met:

1. The requested change is listed as an automatic change with a designated change number (DCN)
2. The taxpayer provides all required information on Form 3115
3. The taxpayer computes and includes the 481(a) adjustment
4. The taxpayer is not under examination for the item (with exceptions — see examination limitations below)
5. The taxpayer has not made or requested a change for the same item within the prior **5 tax years** (the "5-year prior change restriction"), unless an exception applies
6. The taxpayer is not before the IRS Appeals or a federal court on the same method issue
7. For changes with a limited window period, the change is filed within the window

### 5-Year Prior Change Restriction

A taxpayer who changed (or applied to change) the same item within the last 5 tax years is generally ineligible for automatic consent for that item. Exceptions:

- Changes required by new legislation or regulations
- Changes from an impermissible to a permissible method (even if within 5 years of a prior change)
- Specific DCNs that waive the 5-year restriction

### Window Period

Certain automatic changes have a limited filing window. Under Rev. Proc. 2015-13 Section 5.05, some changes must be made within the first or second tax year after a specific event (e.g., new legislation effective date). The window period varies by DCN — check the specific change number provisions.

### Under Examination Limitations (Section 7)

If the taxpayer's return for the year of change (or any prior year) is under examination:

- **General rule**: Automatic consent is unavailable for the specific item under examination
- **90-day window exception**: A change may be filed within the first 90 days of a tax year that begins while the prior year is under examination, provided the change relates to a different item than what is being examined
- **Audit CAP exception**: The examining agent may permit the change under the audit change procedures (but the agent controls whether audit protection applies)
- **Appeals/court limitation**: If the item is before Appeals or a federal court, automatic consent is unavailable

## Non-Automatic (Advance) Consent Procedures

### When Required

Non-automatic consent is required when:

- The change is not listed in the automatic consent procedures
- The taxpayer is ineligible for automatic consent (under examination, 5-year restriction without exception)
- The IRS has specifically excluded the change from automatic treatment

### Filing Procedure

1. Prepare Form 3115 with Part II completed
2. Include a detailed legal analysis explaining why the present method is impermissible or why the proposed method is preferable
3. Compute the 481(a) adjustment
4. Submit to the IRS National Office with the applicable user fee
5. The IRS will issue a letter ruling granting or denying consent, potentially with conditions or modifications

### User Fee

The IRS publishes the user fee schedule annually (Rev. Proc. 2025-1 or successor). For method changes:

- Standard user fee: $12,600 (2025)
- Reduced fee for gross receipts under $1M: $3,000
- Reduced fee for individuals with AGI under $250K: $3,000

### Processing Timeline

Non-automatic requests typically take 6-12 months for IRS processing. Plan accordingly — if the ruling is not received before the return due date (including extensions), the taxpayer may need to file based on the present method and amend upon receipt of the ruling.

## Audit Protection — Section 11.01

### Scope of Protection

Under Rev. Proc. 2015-13 Section 11.01, a taxpayer who properly files Form 3115 under the automatic consent procedures receives **audit protection** for the use of the method being changed in prior tax years. This means:

- The IRS will not require the taxpayer to change back to the old method for any prior year
- The IRS will not adjust prior returns to reflect the new method for years before the year of change
- The 481(a) adjustment captures the entire cumulative effect of the method difference, so prior years need not be reopened

### Conditions for Audit Protection

Audit protection applies when:

1. The Form 3115 is filed timely under automatic consent
2. The taxpayer complies with all terms and conditions of the change
3. The 481(a) adjustment is properly computed and reported
4. The change is from an impermissible method to a permissible method, OR between permissible methods as authorized

### When Audit Protection Does NOT Apply

- The change is filed under non-automatic consent (audit protection is at IRS discretion in the ruling letter)
- The taxpayer fails to comply with the procedural requirements
- The Form 3115 is filed as part of an audit resolution (audit CAP procedures — the examining agent determines whether protection applies)
- The IRS specifically excludes audit protection for a particular DCN or circumstance

### Strategic Significance

Audit protection is often the primary motivation for filing Form 3115 voluntarily. A taxpayer using an impermissible method who self-identifies and files Form 3115 receives protection for all prior open years. If instead the IRS discovers the impermissible method on examination, the IRS can require the change AND adjust prior open years — a significantly worse outcome.

## Duplicate Filing Requirements

### Automatic Changes

Two copies of Form 3115 are required:

1. **Original** — Attached to the timely filed federal income tax return (including extensions) for the year of change
2. **Duplicate** — Sent to the IRS National Office at the designated address (currently Ogden, UT) no later than the date the original is filed with the return

### Non-Automatic Changes

Only one copy is required — filed with the IRS National Office. The taxpayer should reference the pending application on the return for the year of change and report the method change consistent with the application.

### Electronic Filing Considerations

When the return is e-filed, Form 3115 is attached as a PDF. The duplicate must still be mailed separately to the IRS National Office.

## Common Procedural Errors

- Filing the Form 3115 for the wrong year of change (it must be the first year the new method is used)
- Omitting the duplicate filing for automatic changes
- Failing to include the 481(a) adjustment computation
- Using the wrong DCN for the intended change
- Filing under automatic consent when the taxpayer is ineligible (under examination for the item)
- Missing the 5-year prior change restriction without verifying whether an exception applies
- Not attaching the applicable schedule (A through E) for the type of change
- Filing a non-automatic request without the user fee
