## IRS Collection Process Overview

The collection sequence after a tax return is filed (or assessed via SFR/audit):

1. **Assessment**: Tax liability is recorded on the taxpayer's account (assessment date starts the 10-year Collection Statute Expiration Date — CSED)
2. **Notice and demand**: IRS sends initial balance due notice (CP14 for return-filed balances)
3. **Escalating notices**: If unpaid, IRS sends a series of notices at approximately 30-60 day intervals
4. **Federal tax lien**: Arises automatically upon assessment + demand + failure to pay. Notice of Federal Tax Lien (NFTL) may be filed publicly
5. **Levy authority**: After proper notice and waiting periods, IRS may seize property, wages, and bank accounts

### 10-Year Collection Statute (CSED)

The IRS generally has **10 years from the date of assessment** to collect a tax debt (IRC 6502). After the CSED expires, the debt is legally uncollectible. The CSED can be extended or suspended by:
- Installment agreement (suspended during IA plus 30 days after termination)
- Offer in compromise (suspended during OIC consideration plus 30 days)
- CDP hearing request (suspended during hearing)
- Bankruptcy (suspended during automatic stay plus 6 months)
- Taxpayer living outside the US for 6+ continuous months
- Military service in a combat zone
- Voluntary waiver on Form 900

## IRS Notice Sequence

| Notice | Title | Action Required | Deadline |
|--------|-------|-----------------|----------|
| CP14 | Balance Due | Pay, dispute, or request payment plan | 21 days (30 if amount > $100K) |
| CP501 | Reminder of Balance Due | Same as CP14 — second request | 30 days |
| CP503 | Urgent — Amount Still Due | Pay or contact IRS; final standard reminder | 30 days |
| CP504 | Intent to Levy (state refund, certain payments) | Pay or call; levies limited property types | 30 days |
| LT11 | Final Notice — Intent to Levy and Right to CDP Hearing | Request CDP hearing to stop levy | **30 days** (critical) |
| LT14 | Notice of Federal Tax Lien Filing — Right to CDP Hearing | Request CDP hearing to challenge lien | **30 days** (critical) |
| CP90 | Final Notice of Intent to Levy (alternative format) | Request CDP hearing | **30 days** |
| CP297 | Final Notice of Intent to Levy (for businesses) | Request CDP hearing | **30 days** |

**Note**: LT11/CP90/CP297 and LT14 trigger Collection Due Process (CDP) rights. Missing the 30-day window forfeits the right to a CDP hearing (only an equivalent hearing is available, without Tax Court petition rights).

## Federal Tax Lien

### Automatic Lien (IRC 6321)

A federal tax lien arises **automatically** when:
1. Tax is assessed
2. Notice and demand for payment is sent
3. Taxpayer fails to pay within 10 days

The lien attaches to **all property and rights to property** of the taxpayer — real estate, personal property, financial assets, accounts receivable, inventory.

### Notice of Federal Tax Lien (NFTL)

Filing the NFTL is **discretionary** — it makes the lien public record. Impacts:
- Credit rating (reported to credit bureaus)
- Clouds title on real property
- Gives IRS priority over subsequent creditors
- Required before IRS can levy certain property

### Lien Release and Withdrawal

- **Release**: IRS must release the lien within 30 days of full payment or when the CSED expires (IRC 6325)
- **Withdrawal**: Removes the public filing as if never filed. Available when: IA is established (for balances under $25,000 with direct debit), filing was premature, or withdrawal facilitates collection
- **Subordination/discharge**: Available for specific property transactions (refinancing, sale) — IRS subordinates its lien position or discharges the lien from specific property

## Levy

### Authority (IRC 6331)

After providing a **final notice of intent to levy** and waiting **30 days** (CDP period), the IRS may seize bank accounts (one-time freeze, 21-day hold), wages (continuous levy), accounts receivable, Social Security benefits (up to 15%), state tax refunds, and real property (requires additional approval and 30-day pre-sale notice).

**Exempt property (IRC 6334)**: Necessary clothing/school books, personal effects up to $10,090 (2025), tools of trade up to $5,040 (2025), unemployment/workers comp benefits, undelivered mail, certain pension payments, child support, and minimum exempt wages (based on filing status/exemptions).

**Release**: IRS must release when tax is paid, CSED expires, levy creates economic hardship, IA is established, FMV exceeds liability and release won't hinder collection, or appeal is pending.

## Offer in Compromise (OIC)

### Three Grounds

1. **Doubt as to Liability (DATL)**: Dispute assessed tax. Form 656-L, no application fee or payment
2. **Doubt as to Collectibility (DATC)**: Cannot pay full amount (most common). Form 656, $205 fee (low-income waived)
3. **Effective Tax Administration (ETA)**: Can pay but would create hardship or be inequitable. Form 656

### Reasonable Collection Potential (RCP)

The OIC must equal or exceed **RCP = Net Equity in Assets + (Monthly Disposable Income x Future Income Multiplier)**. Net equity: FMV minus encumbrances x 80% quick-sale discount. Disposable income: gross income minus IRS-allowed living expenses. Multiplier: 12 months (lump sum, paid within 5 months) or 24 months (periodic payment, 6-24 months).

### Payment and Processing

- **Lump sum**: 20% nonrefundable with Form 656; remainder within 5 months of acceptance
- **Periodic**: First monthly payment with Form 656; continue payments while pending (nonrefundable)
- **Low-income**: Application fee and initial payments waived
- IRS has 24 months to decide (deemed accepted if no decision). All returns must be filed; estimated taxes current while pending. Accepted OIC requires 5 years of compliance or default.

## Currently Not Collectible (CNC)

When the taxpayer demonstrates **inability to pay** any amount toward the tax debt (income covers only necessary living expenses with no remaining disposable income).

- IRS suspends active collection — no levies, no IA demands
- The **10-year CSED continues running** — debt may expire before the taxpayer's financial situation improves
- IRS will periodically review the account (typically annually) to reassess ability to pay
- Filing a NFTL is still possible while in CNC status
- Requires Form 433-A/433-B financial disclosure

## Collection Due Process (CDP) — IRC 6320/6330

CDP rights arise upon receipt of a NFTL filing notice (IRC 6320) or final levy notice (IRC 6330) — **30 days** to request a hearing.

**Hearing scope**: Appropriateness of collection action, proposed alternatives (IA, OIC, CNC), spousal defenses, and challenge to underlying liability (only if no prior opportunity to dispute, e.g., no statutory notice of deficiency received).

**Tax Court**: If CDP determination is adverse, taxpayer has 30 days to petition U.S. Tax Court — the only judicial review path for collection disputes. If the 30-day CDP window is missed, an **equivalent hearing** is available within 1 year but carries no Tax Court petition right.

## Penalty Abatement in Collections Context

See `notice-response.md` for detailed FTA and reasonable cause rules. In collections: request **FTA** first (largest penalty year), then **reasonable cause** for remaining years. Penalty abatement reduces the total balance, which may qualify the taxpayer for a streamlined IA (under $50K threshold) or improve OIC acceptance odds by lowering the RCP. Interest on abated penalties is automatically removed. Standalone interest abatement is rare — available only for IRS error/delay (IRC 6404(e)).

## Trust Fund Recovery Penalty (TFRP) — IRC 6672

For employment tax debts, the IRS may assess the TFRP against any **responsible person** who **willfully** failed to collect, account for, and pay over trust fund taxes (employee withholding of income tax and FICA).

- **Responsible person**: Anyone with authority to direct payment of the taxes — officers, directors, shareholders with control, bookkeepers/accountants with check-signing authority
- **Willfulness**: Voluntary, conscious, and intentional — not merely negligent. Knowledge of the outstanding liability coupled with directing payments to other creditors satisfies the standard
- **Amount**: Equal to 100% of the unpaid trust fund portion (does not include the employer share of FICA)
- **Personal liability**: TFRP is assessed against the individual, not just the business entity
- **Investigation**: IRS Revenue Officer conducts Form 4180 interview to determine responsible persons

## Florida DOR Collections

**Assessment sequence**: DR-831 (Notice of Intent to Make Audit Changes, 30 days) → NOPA (Notice of Proposed Assessment, 60 days to protest) → Warrant (judgment equivalent if unpaid).

**Collection powers**: Bank levy (no court order needed), wage garnishment, driver license/vehicle registration suspension (sales tax), warrant lien filing in county records, vendor hold on state agency payments.

**Compromise**: FL DOR may settle debts under Florida Statute 213.21 (doubt as to collectibility or liability). Same factors as IRS OIC. Submit to FL DOR Technical Assistance and Dispute Resolution.

**Interest and penalties**: Interest at prime + 4% (adjusted Jan 1 and Jul 1, F.S. 220.807). Late filing: 10% per month, max 50%. Late payment: 10%. Abatement available for reasonable cause.

## Decision Points

1. **Which resolution path?** Determine ability to pay first (Form 433-B analysis): full pay → pay in full; partial pay capability → installment agreement; no ability to pay → CNC or OIC
2. **Streamlined or regular IA?** If combined balance is $50,000 or less, streamlined IA avoids financial disclosure. If above $50,000, full financials are required
3. **Is an OIC viable?** Compute RCP — if the OIC amount would be less than the total balance, it may be accepted. OIC is rejected if the taxpayer can full-pay through an IA within the CSED
4. **Should CDP be requested?** Always request a CDP hearing when levy or lien notices are received — it stops collection and preserves Tax Court rights
5. **Is penalty abatement available?** Request FTA first (largest penalty year), then reasonable cause for remaining years. Abatement reduces the balance before negotiating IA/OIC
6. **Is FL DOR a separate collection issue?** FL DOR collections proceed independently. Resolve federal and state collections in parallel

## Accounting System Integration Notes

- **Tax liability tracking**: Record assessed tax, penalties, and interest as a liability (Income Tax Payable or a dedicated "Tax Debt" liability account). Update as payments are made. Delegate journal entry execution to `qbo-integration:qbo-bookkeeping`.
- **IA payments**: Record monthly installment payments as debits to the tax liability account and credits to Cash. Track principal vs interest/penalty portions if the IRS provides a breakdown.
- **OIC settlement**: When an OIC is accepted, write off the difference between the balance and the OIC amount as a gain (Other Income — Tax Debt Forgiveness) or reduce the liability directly. Note: forgiven tax debt is not taxable income for the debtor corporation (unlike canceled debt under Section 108).
- **Penalty abatement**: When penalties are abated, reduce the tax liability account by the abated amount. Interest on abated penalties is automatically removed — record that reduction as well.
- **Accrued interest**: IRS interest accrues daily. If the corporation's books need to reflect the full current balance, accrue interest quarterly based on the IRS underpayment rate.
